# Workload Planning

A practice runs on two artifacts: well-cut slices and an honest capacity
picture. This playbook maintains both.

## Steps

1. **Cut workstreams into finishable slices.** Each slice: one owner, its own
   acceptance criteria, no hidden dependency on an unfinished slice, and a
   size one engineer completes without renegotiating scope mid-flight. If a
   slice needs two practices, it is two slices with a written interface
   between them.
2. **Match slices to fluency, then to growth.** First question: who is fluent
   in this stack? Second: who should become fluent, and can this slice afford
   the learning curve? Assignment by mere availability is recorded as a risk,
   not hidden as a plan.
3. **Keep a visible capacity ledger.** Per engineer: current slices, review
   load, and the interrupt tax (support, incidents) — planned at honest
   capacity, not theoretical. Review time is scheduled work; a practice that
   queues reviews is a practice shipping unreviewed risk later.
4. **Sequence by risk-information yield.** Slices that retire the biggest
   unknowns run first, even when easier wins are available — bad news early
   is cheaper than bad news late. Blocked slices escalate the day they block,
   with what is needed and from whom.
5. **Re-forecast weekly, out loud.** Shipped, in-flight, blocked, at-risk —
   compared against the commitment. When capacity and commitment diverge, the
   lead says so upward immediately with options (cut scope, move date, add
   fluency), never discovering it at the deadline.
6. **Close slices with evidence.** A slice is done when its acceptance
   criteria have passing verification and its handoff note exists — not when
   its author feels finished.

## Quality bar

- No engineer holds two must-finish-first slices at once.
- Every blocked slice has an escalation timestamp under a day old.
- The weekly status names risks by slice, not by mood.
- Fluency-mismatch assignments are explicit, with mitigation stated.

## Output format

A living practice board (slices, owners, states) plus the weekly status:
shipped, blocked, at-risk, capacity deltas, and any commitment renegotiation
proposed.
