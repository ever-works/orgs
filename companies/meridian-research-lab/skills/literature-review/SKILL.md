# Literature Review

Produce an annotated evidence map organized by claim, not by source. The map must let a reader see in one glance where the field agrees, where it fights, and where it is silent.

## Procedure

1. **Restate the question.** Write the survey question as one sentence and list 3-6 sub-questions the map must answer. If you cannot, return the task for clarification before searching.
2. **Cast wide.** Search across source types: surveys and meta-analyses first, then primary studies, then credible practitioner and industry reports, then datasets. Log every search avenue you tried, including the empty ones.
3. **Filter hard.** Keep a source only if it bears directly on a sub-question. For each exclusion of a plausible-looking source, record one line: source, reason excluded.
4. **Grade every kept source** on five axes: methodology strength, sample or scope size, recency, independence from interested parties, and replication status. Use a simple strong / moderate / weak grade with a one-line justification.
5. **Extract claims, not summaries.** For each source, pull the specific assertions relevant to the sub-questions. One claim per line, in your own words, with the source and grade attached.
6. **Cluster by claim.** Group extracted assertions so that supporting and contradicting sources for the same claim sit together. Never average a disagreement into a mushy middle — show both sides with their grades.
7. **Assess consensus per claim:** established (multiple independent strong sources, no credible contradiction), contested (credible sources disagree), or unknown (no strong source addresses it).
8. **Declare saturation.** When new searches stop changing the map, stop. Note the stopping point explicitly.
9. **Write the gaps section.** List what the literature cannot answer — these gaps are candidate questions for direct measurement.

## Quality bar

- Every claim traces to a named, graded source in one hop.
- Contradictions are displayed, prominent, and unresolved unless the evidence genuinely resolves them.
- No quotation-stitching: all summaries are original prose.
- The exclusion log and search log are present — a map without them is not auditable.

## Output format

A single document: (1) question and sub-questions, (2) claim clusters with graded sources for/against and a consensus label each, (3) gaps, (4) search log, (5) exclusion log. Lead with a five-line summary of the strongest established claims and the most important contested one.
