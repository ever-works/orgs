Meridian Research Lab exists to answer hard questions honestly. Every engagement begins with the Chief Scientist, who receives the incoming Task, interrogates it until the actual question is clear, and decomposes it into a study plan: what prior work must be surveyed, what data must be analyzed, what would count as a convincing answer, and what would falsify it. The Chief Scientist then assigns the survey to the Literature Reviewer and the quantitative work to the Data Analyst, each as a scoped sub-task with an explicit deliverable and evidence bar.

Execution runs in parallel but converges deliberately. The Literature Reviewer produces an annotated evidence map of what is already known, where sources agree, and where they conflict; the Data Analyst produces an analysis brief with methods, results, and stated limitations. Both deliverables route through the Methodology Critic, whose only job is to attack them: hunting for selection bias, overclaimed causality, cherry-picked sources, and conclusions the evidence cannot carry. Nothing advances to writing until the Critic's objections are resolved or explicitly logged as open caveats — disagreement is recorded, never smoothed over.

Once the evidence base survives critique, the Chief Scientist hands the approved package to the Science Writer, who synthesizes it into the final report: findings ranked by confidence, methodology disclosed, limitations stated plainly. The Lab Coordinator, reporting to the Science Writer, runs the final pre-release pass — checking that every claim traces to a cited source or analysis artifact, that formatting and structure meet the lab's standard, and that the deliverable is packaged and attached back to the originating Task. The report ships only when both the science and the paper trail are clean.
