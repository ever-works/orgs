# Campaign Brief Review

The brief is the contract every deliverable is judged against, so reviewing the brief is the highest-leverage review in the agency. A weak brief fails quietly, weeks later, across every channel at once.

## Reviewing a brief before sign-off

Walk these checks in order; stop and send back at the first hard failure.

1. **Falsifiable objective.** One sentence naming a business outcome you could later verify happened or didn't. "Raise awareness" fails; "generate a stated number of qualified signups from a named segment by a named date" passes.
2. **Real audience.** Who exactly, what they currently believe, and what the campaign should make them believe or do. If the audience section would fit any company's campaign, it's decoration.
3. **One message.** The single takeaway, in one sentence, no "and". Two messages means two campaigns — split them now, cheaply, instead of at review time, expensively.
4. **Credible support.** The few reasons the message is believable, each marked as verified fact or open assumption. Unmarked assumptions become public claims later.
5. **Complete deliverables list.** Every asset with format, channel, owner role, and order. This list defines campaign-done; missing entries surface as "quick asks" mid-campaign.
6. **Stated constraints.** Deadline, budget, mandatory elements, claims to avoid, channel restrictions. Hunt the unstated ones — they always exist, and they surface as rejections if not written down.
7. **Success measure with a date.** How the objective will be checked, with what data, and when. A campaign that can't fail can't teach anything either.

## The stranger test

Read only the message and the deliverables list: could a competent stranger produce roughly the right campaign? If yes, the brief works. If no, the middle sections are prose, not instruction.

## Reviewing a deliverable against a brief

- Identify the brief line the deliverable claims to answer. No line, no deliverable — that's scope creep to challenge, not a bonus to accept.
- Check the deliverable carries the single message; a technically excellent asset carrying a different message is a rejection.
- Verify claims against the support section; any claim not in it needs a source or a cut.
- Reject in writing, citing the specific brief line or measure violated. "Doesn't feel right" is a conversation starter, not a verdict.

## Quality bar

- One page. Briefs nobody reads protect nobody.
- Versioned: post-sign-off changes get a new version and a one-line changelog so everyone knows which brief is live.
- Signed briefs are dated and named — an unsigned brief authorizes nothing.
