# Content Calendar Planning

The calendar is the agency's single source of truth for what publishes, where, and when. Work not on the calendar does not exist; work on the calendar has an owner, a format, and a job.

## Building the calendar from a brief

1. **List the jobs.** From the brief, extract what content must accomplish: intents to own, questions to answer, moments to hit (launch date, event, seasonal window). Each job becomes one or more entries.
2. **One row per piece.** Every entry gets: working title, format (article, landing page, email, post series, asset), channel, target intent or audience question, owner role, dependencies, and publish order.
3. **Anchor first, satellites after.** Schedule the big pieces (long-form, landing pages) before the pieces that promote or slice them. A satellite scheduled before its anchor is a dependency bug.
4. **Respect production reality.** Walk the dependency chain backwards from each publish date: review time, design time, drafting time. If the chain doesn't fit, move the date now — not the week it slips.
5. **Mark the seams.** Flag every entry another team feeds (SEO intent input, design assets, social adaptation) so handoffs are visible in the plan, not discovered at publish time.

## Sequencing rules

- Never schedule two pieces chasing the same query or the same audience moment; merge or re-target one.
- Space repeat formats so no channel gets monotone weeks — vary format even when the message repeats.
- Leave deliberate slack: an unplanned-content slot per cycle for reactive opportunities beats breaking the schedule for them.

## Keeping it alive

- The calendar is versioned. Every change after sign-off gets a one-line changelog entry: what moved and why.
- Fold in audience-reaction notes and performance data on a fixed rhythm — revise the plan when the data says to, and record the data that said so.
- A slipped entry is rescheduled explicitly or cut explicitly. Entries never silently drift.

## Quality bar

- A stranger reading only the calendar could say what publishes next week, who owes what, and why each piece exists.
- Every entry traces to a brief line; anything that can't is scope creep and gets challenged.
- The changelog tells the story of the campaign's contact with reality — an unchanged calendar after four weeks of publishing is a red flag, not a badge.
