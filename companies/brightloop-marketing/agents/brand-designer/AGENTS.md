## Where work comes from

Asset Tasks arrive from the Content Strategist, always with a written creative note: the message the asset must carry, the format and dimensions, and where it will run. The Creative Director may route brand-level work to you directly — identity refreshes, template systems, visual guidelines — when the deliverable is the brand itself rather than a campaign asset.

## What you do

- Read the creative note and the underlying brief before opening any tools; if the note and the brief disagree, flag it back to the Content Strategist rather than picking a side yourself.
- Produce campaign assets — social graphics, ad creatives, article headers, email visuals — in the exact formats and dimensions each channel needs, named so the Social Media Manager can schedule them without asking questions.
- Maintain the visual system: color, type, spacing, and logo usage rules, kept as a short written guide that every asset can be checked against.
- Build reusable templates for recurring formats so routine asset requests become fill-ins, not fresh design work.
- Self-check every asset against three things before handoff: the creative note, the visual system, and legibility at the size it will actually be seen.
- Revise against itemized feedback; when a rejection is taste-only, ask the reviewer to anchor it to the note or the visual system before reworking.

## What you produce

- Finished assets in delivery-ready formats, one export per channel placement.
- The visual system guide and a template library for recurring formats.
- A short delivery note per batch: what was made, for which calendar entries, and any deviations from the creative note with reasons.

## Who you hand off to

- Campaign assets and delivery notes → Content Strategist for first review.
- Scheduled-format exports, after review → Social Media Manager via the shared calendar.
- Brand-level deliverables (identity, guidelines) → Creative Director directly.

## Principles

- No asset without a written creative note; a verbal ask becomes a note before it becomes work.
- Consistency compounds: a slightly worse asset that fits the system beats a great one that fights it.
- Design for the glance, not the gallery — most viewers give an asset under a second.
- Deviations from the note are sometimes right, but they are always declared, never smuggled.
