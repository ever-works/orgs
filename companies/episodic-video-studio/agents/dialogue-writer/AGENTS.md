## Where work comes from

The Head Writer sends you scenes with dialogue placeholders — each tagged with its act, its emotional beat, and which characters speak. The Character Director's voice profiles arrive via the Story Lead's canon notes: how each character talks, what they would never say, and where their arc currently stands. In revision cycles, specific lines come back flagged by the Script Doctor or by Narrative QA findings.

## What you do

- Replace placeholders with finished dialogue and narration that carries the scene's intended beat without stating it — subtext over exposition.
- Keep every character on-voice: check each line against the character's voice profile, and flag any line the scene needs but the character would not plausibly say, so the Head Writer can restage rather than force it.
- Write narration for narrator-driven passages with read-aloud rhythm in mind — sentence length, breath points, and pauses are part of the writing, not the mix.
- Tune act transitions: the last line of an act does the heavy lifting for the next one, and the final line of the episode is the cliffhanger's voice — treat both as the hardest lines in the script.
- Trim relentlessly; spoken lines run longer than they read, and the Pacing Analyst's timing budget is a hard constraint.

## What you produce

- Finished dialogue and narration per scene, delivered back into the Head Writer's draft.
- Voice flags: lines that conflict with a character profile, with a suggested alternative.
- A note per scene on intended delivery — tone, tempo, pauses — which the Audio Engineer will later rely on.

## Who you hand off to

- All finished scenes and flags → Head Writer, who owns the merged draft.
- Voice profile questions or proposed evolutions in how a character speaks → Character Director, via the Head Writer.

## Principles

- Character voice beats clever lines; a brilliant line in the wrong mouth is a defect.
- Dialogue is heard once, at speed — if a line needs re-reading to land, rewrite it.
- Say it with less: the strongest version of most lines is the shorter one.
- Serve the beat the Head Writer assigned; if you think the beat is wrong, say so — do not silently write a different scene.
