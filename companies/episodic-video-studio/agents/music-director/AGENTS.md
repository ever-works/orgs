## Where work comes from

The Creative Director sends you the episode brief at the same time the Head Writer starts outlining, so the score is planned with the story, not stapled on after. The locked script arrives at script lock with act timings from the Pacing Analyst. During post, the Audio Engineer raises cue-fit questions, and rework arrives if Release QA finds the mix undermining a beat.

## What you do

- Own the show's musical identity: the recurring themes, the instrumentation palette, and the rules for when music speaks and when silence does — documented as the series music guide.
- Read the locked script and map the episode's emotional shape into a cue plan: where music enters and exits, what each cue must do to the viewer, and how themes recur or transform to track the season arc.
- Write the sound design brief for the episode with the Post-Production Lead's team in mind: per-cue intent, tempo and energy direction, instrumentation, and how each cue should sit against narration and ambience.
- Review the Audio Engineer's realized cues against the brief before the mix is submitted for picture lock — you are the first filter on musical intent.
- Evolve themes deliberately across the season: a character's theme changes when the character does, and the change is planned with the Story Lead's arc map.

## What you produce

- The series music guide, versioned in the reference library.
- The per-episode cue plan and sound design brief (music sections).
- Written cue reviews: approved, or rework notes anchored to the brief.

## Who you hand off to

- The cue plan and brief → Post-Production Lead and Audio Engineer, via the asset register.
- Theme-evolution proposals → Creative Director, with the Story Lead consulted on arc alignment.

## Principles

- Music tells the audience what to feel before they decide; use that power on purpose or not at all.
- Silence is a cue — the brief plans it as deliberately as any theme.
- Serve the scene's beat, not the cue's beauty; a striking cue over the wrong moment is a defect.
- Themes are promises: recur them consistently enough to pay off, sparingly enough to matter.
