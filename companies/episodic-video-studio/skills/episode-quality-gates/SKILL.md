# Episode Quality Gates

An episode crosses a stage boundary only through a gate. A gate is a written checklist applied by an independent reviewer; it exists to catch defects while they are still cheap.

## The three gates

1. **Script lock** — ends the writing stage. Pass criteria: the five-act structure holds with act boundaries findable in the text; canon annotations are satisfied; characters act within their profiles; the episode does the job the greenlight note assigned it in the season arc; the cliffhanger states one clean open question; the timing budget survives estimation. After script lock the text is frozen — downstream stages build on it, they do not rewrite it.
2. **Visual lock** — ends the visual planning stage. Pass criteria: every script scene has board coverage serving its beat; representative stills match their panels in composition, pose, and light; recurring characters and locations are on-model against the reference library; sequences hold screen direction and light continuity; the prompt set actually reproduces the approved stills.
3. **Release lock** — the final gate. Pass criteria: the finished episode survives a cold, full-attention viewing without an immersion break; the technical floor holds (mix standard, no glitches, chapters on act boundaries, runtime in tolerance); the post change log is complete and none of its deviations broke an earlier gate's approval; the release package is honest — title, thumbnail, and description promise what the episode delivers and spoil nothing.

## Submission rules

- A submission is complete or it is inadmissible: the work, its brief, its references, and — on resubmission — a change log since the last verdict. Incomplete submissions bounce without review.
- The maker's lead reviews before the gate does; the gate is the second filter, never the first.

## Verdict rules

- Two outcomes only: pass, or an itemized rejection. No conditional passes — "pass with notes" means the notes are advisory and the pass is real.
- Every rejection finding ties to a written criterion and a location in the work. A finding that cites neither gets bounced back to the reviewer.
- Resubmissions re-review changed sections fully and spot-check the rest; fixes break things elsewhere often enough to make this rule pay for itself.
- Disputes go up, not sideways: application-of-criteria disputes to the gate owner, criteria-are-wrong disputes to the top of the org. Verdicts never change under schedule pressure — only under fixed work or an overruled criterion, in writing.

## Reading gate history

- Track rework cycles per episode and finding categories per gate. A gate that fails the same way three times is diagnosing an upstream process defect; fix the process, then the criteria if needed.
- Pass rates trending toward 100% with defects appearing downstream means the gate has gone soft — audit it.
