# Five-Act Episode Outline

Every episode is one emotional journey with five acts. The outline is written and approved before any scene is drafted, because scene-level polish cannot repair act-level failure.

## The five acts

1. **Hook.** The opening seconds. One striking image, question, or situation that earns attention honestly — it must be paid off inside this episode, or it is clickbait in script form. No throat-clearing, no recap longer than one breath.
2. **Setup.** Establish where we are, who we are with, and what this episode is about — while the story is already moving. Setup delivered by motion beats setup delivered by explanation. End the act with the episode's question made concrete.
3. **Escalation.** The longest act. Complications compound, stakes rise, and each scene changes something: knowledge, resources, relationships, or danger. The audit test per scene — what is different after it? A scene with no answer is a cut candidate.
4. **Climax.** The episode's question gets its answer, and the answer costs something. The payoff must use what Setup planted; a climax resolved by something never established is a structural defect, not a twist.
5. **Cliffhanger.** A new, genuine open question created by the climax's outcome — not an arbitrary interruption. The final image and final line are the episode's most expensive real estate; write them last, cut them hardest.

## Building the outline

- One sentence per act first: if the five sentences do not make a story, more detail will not save it.
- Assign a timing budget per act as a share of target runtime; Escalation gets the largest share, the Hook the smallest.
- List every setup planted and every payoff owed — the two lists must reconcile within the episode or be explicitly assigned to the season arc.
- Mark which act carries the episode's character-arc step, so it is designed rather than hoped for.

## Auditing a draft against this structure

- Locate the act boundaries in the actual text. If you cannot find one, the draft drifted from the outline — flag it.
- Check each act does its one job and hands off cleanly: the end of every act should make stopping feel impossible.
- Verify the timing budget survived drafting; a Setup that swallowed a third of the runtime is a pacing finding.
- Test the cliffhanger by stating the open question aloud in one sentence. If it needs two, it is a mush, not a hook.

## Output format

A one-page outline: five acts, one paragraph and one timing figure each, the setup/payoff ledger, and the character-arc step — signed off before drafting begins.
