# Storyboard Brief

The storyboard is the proof that an episode works visually before any frame is generated. It is a plan drawn for clarity, not an artwork drawn for admiration.

## From script to shot list

- Walk the locked script scene by scene and ask, per beat: what must the viewer see to feel this? The answer is a shot; the beat is the shot's stated intent.
- Default to economy: the fewest shots that carry the scene. Coverage for its own sake bloats generation cost and dilutes the cut.
- Vary shot value deliberately — distance, angle, and duration are the emotional dials; a scene of same-size shots is a flatline regardless of content.
- Mark the scene's key frame: the single image the scene exists to deliver. If no shot qualifies, the scene is not designed yet.

## Panel requirements

Each panel carries:
- **IDs**: stable shot ID and the script scene it serves — downstream references hang on these, so they never change once assigned.
- **Composition**: the frame as it will be framed — subject placement, camera height and angle, movement arrows for camera or subject.
- **Light**: source and direction indicated; light continuity errors are the most common sequence defect.
- **Intent**: one line — what this shot does to the viewer.
- **Duration estimate**: feeds the pacing check against act timings.
- **Continuity-critical details**: the held prop, the wound, the weather — flagged so no later stage drops them.

## Sequence rules

- Boards are judged as sequences: check screen direction, eyelines, and the 180-degree line across every cut within a scene.
- Every panel must cut from its predecessor and into its successor; a panel that only works alone is a poster.
- The transition into and out of each scene is designed, not defaulted — scene boundaries are where attention leaks.

## Review pass before sign-off

- Read the boards without the script: does the story read? If key beats vanish without the text, the boards are decorating the script, not translating it.
- Reconcile total estimated duration against the act timing budget.
- Verify every continuity-critical annotation from the script survived onto a panel.

## Output format

Panels in sequence order per scene, the panel index listing every shot ID with its scene and duration, and a one-page summary of key frames per act.
