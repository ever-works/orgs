# Sound Design Brief

Sound tells the audience what to feel before they decide for themselves. The brief plans that influence cue by cue — including the silences — so the mix realizes intent instead of improvising it.

## Structure of the brief

1. **Episode sound statement.** Two or three sentences: the episode's emotional shape in audio terms — where the sound leans in, where it withdraws, what the ending should leave ringing.
2. **Cue plan.** For every music entrance and exit: the timestamp anchor (scene and beat, since exact times shift with the cut), the cue's job stated as an effect on the viewer ("unease building under false calm", not "tense strings"), energy direction (building, sustaining, releasing), instrumentation from the show's palette, and which recurring theme it uses or varies, if any.
3. **Silence plan.** The moments that must carry no music, listed as deliberately as the cues — with the reason, so nobody "fixes" them in the mix.
4. **Narration treatment.** How the voice sits per act: intimacy and distance, pacing and pause discipline from the script's delivery notes, and where narration yields to pure sound.
5. **Ambience map.** Each location's sonic signature — consistent with how that location has sounded before — plus the transitions between them, which carry the scene handoffs.

## Theme discipline

- Recurring themes are promises: they attach to characters, places, or ideas, and recur with meaning, not as wallpaper.
- A theme changes when its subject changes — planned against the season arc, never on mix-day whim.
- New themes are introduced sparingly; a show with twenty themes has none.

## Reviewing a realized mix against the brief

- Check each cue against its stated job at the placed position: does the moment do what the brief said it must?
- Verify the silence plan survived — filled silence is the most common and most damaging deviation.
- Confirm narration intelligibility everywhere; no cue's beauty outranks a lost line.
- Deviations driven by the actual cut are legitimate but travel as questions to the brief's owner, not as silent fixes.

## Quality bar

- Every cue has a written job; a cue nobody can justify is clutter with a melody.
- The brief is readable by someone who has only the script: anchors are scenes and beats, not minute marks.
- Consistency across episodes is part of the design — the show must sound like itself in a blind test.
