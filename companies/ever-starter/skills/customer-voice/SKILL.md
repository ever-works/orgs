# Customer Voice

Customers tell you what to build, how to sell it, and what words to use — but only if their words survive contact with your paraphrasing. This skill is the discipline of keeping them intact.

## Steps

1. **Capture verbatim first.** When a customer or prospect describes a problem, log their exact words in quotation marks before writing any summary. The summary is yours; the quote is data. Note the source and context (support reply, sales call, review, survey) and the date.
2. **Separate the layers.** For each captured item keep three things distinct: what they said (quote), what they were doing when it came up (context), and what you think it means (interpretation, clearly labeled as yours).
3. **Tag against a small controlled list.** Maintain roughly five to ten standing themes (e.g. onboarding-confusion, pricing-question, missing-feature-X). Resist inventing a new tag per item — a new tag is justified only when three or more items refuse to fit existing ones.
4. **Rank by frequency and weight.** Periodically count items per theme. Weight prospects who walked away and customers who churned more heavily than casual remarks — pain that cost money outranks pain that got shrugged off.
5. **Put quotes to work.**
   - Copy and messaging: lift the most common phrasing into headlines and FAQs — customers recognize their own words.
   - Product decisions: attach the two or three strongest verbatims to any feature argument; a quote beats an assertion.
   - Sales: mirror the prospect's vocabulary back; never correct their terminology mid-pitch.
6. **Flag extrapolation honestly.** When you generalize from few data points, say so: "based on 3 mentions" is credible; silent extrapolation from one loud customer is how companies chase ghosts.

## Quality bar

- Every claim about "what customers want" traces to at least one dated verbatim quote.
- Quotes are never trimmed in ways that change meaning; ellipses only for length.
- Theme counts are real counts, not impressions.
- Negative and awkward feedback is logged with the same care as praise — especially it.

## Output format

Log entry: `"<exact words>" — <source>, <date> — theme: <tag> — note: <your labeled interpretation>`

Digest (when reporting themes upward): themes ranked by weighted frequency, one representative quote each, plus a single recommended action for the top theme.
