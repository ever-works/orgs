# Weekly Status Report

The report exists so the reader can re-prioritize and unblock. It is not a diary and not a defense of how busy you were.

## Steps

1. **Gather before writing.** List everything that finished, moved, or stalled this week across your tasks and (if you manage) your team's tasks. Pull from the actual task records, not memory.
2. **Sort into four buckets.**
   - **Shipped** — done and accepted. Done means verified, not "sent for review".
   - **In flight** — started, with a realistic expected-done date. Re-date honestly; a slipped date reported early is information, reported late it is a surprise.
   - **Blocked** — cannot proceed without something. Name the something and the specific person or decision that clears it.
   - **Needs decision** — questions only the reader can answer. Phrase each so it can be answered in one line, and attach your recommendation.
3. **Add the numbers that matter.** Two or three metrics relevant to your area, each with last week's value beside it. No metric dumps.
4. **Cut to fit.** The whole report reads in under two minutes. If it does not, you are reporting activity instead of status.
5. **Send on the agreed day, every week** — including bad weeks. An unflattering report on time builds more trust than a polished one that is late.

## Quality bar

- Every blocked item names its unblocking owner; "waiting on stuff" is banned.
- Every needs-decision item includes your recommendation — you are closest to the work.
- No item appears in "in flight" for a third consecutive week without an explanation of why and a changed plan.
- Zero throat-clearing: the first line of the report is already content.

## Output format

```
STATUS — <team/agent> — week of <period>
SHIPPED: <item> (one line each)
IN FLIGHT: <item> — expected <when>
BLOCKED: <item> — needs <what> from <who>
NEEDS DECISION: <question>? — recommend <answer>
NUMBERS: <metric>: <now> (last week: <prev>)
```

Omit empty sections rather than writing "none".
