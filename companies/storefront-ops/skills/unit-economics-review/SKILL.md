# Unit Economics Review

Revenue answers "did people buy"; unit economics answers "did we keep any of it". This review works at four levels — product, order, channel, customer — and the store is only healthy when the math closes at all four.

## Level 1: Product contribution

1. Start from selling price (net of average discount actually given, not list).
2. Subtract landed cost: supplier cost + inbound freight + duties, per unit.
3. Subtract fulfillment: pick/pack, packaging, outbound shipping not paid by the customer.
4. Subtract payment fees and the *returns tax*: return rate × (refund cost + processing + unsellable fraction of returned stock).
5. What remains is **contribution per unit**. This sets the price floor: the price at which contribution reaches zero. Every product has its floor written down; every offer is checked against it.

## Level 2: Order economics

- Average order value, average items per order, and contribution per order (sum of item contributions minus per-order costs counted once).
- Free-shipping thresholds live here: set the threshold so the marginal item a customer adds to qualify contributes more than the shipping you absorb.
- Orders below a contribution floor are a pattern to fix with bundles, thresholds, or minimums — not a customer to resent.

## Level 3: Channel economics

- Contribution per order by acquisition channel, minus that channel's marketing cost per order = channel profit per order.
- A channel can have fine ROAS and terrible economics if it sells low-margin products or attracts discount-only buyers — judge channels on the margin of what they actually sell.
- Blended CAC (total acquisition spend ÷ new customers) is the honesty check on per-channel claims; if every channel looks great and blended looks bad, attribution is lying somewhere.

## Level 4: Customer economics

- Cohort contribution: for customers acquired in a given month/channel, total contribution over 3, 6, 12 months. This — not first-order value — is what CAC must beat, with a payback period the company can finance.
- Segment split that matters most: full-price buyers vs. discount-only buyers. A growing discount-only segment means promotions are training the base, and "LTV" projections built on it are fiction.

## Running the review

- Rank products by total contribution and by contribution rate; the quiet losers are high-revenue low-rate products that soak marketing spend.
- For each loser, choose explicitly: reprice, re-cost (supplier/shipping), restructure (bundle), retire — or keep knowingly as a strategic loss-leader with a written justification and a cap.
- Re-run after anything structural changes: freight rates, supplier costs, return-policy changes, a new discount pattern.
- Present findings as decisions, not spreadsheets: "product X loses money at the current discount cadence; either cap the discount at Y% or retire it."
