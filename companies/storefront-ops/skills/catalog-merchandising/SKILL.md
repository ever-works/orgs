# Catalog Merchandising

The catalog is a ranked argument for what to buy. Merchandising is deciding that ranking on purpose — what gets featured, how products are grouped, what each page recommends next, and when a price moves — and writing the decision down so every channel tells the same story.

## Catalog structure

1. **Collections mirror how customers shop, not how the warehouse is organized.** Build them from search terms, ticket language, and browsing paths: by use case, by problem solved, by occasion — with brand/type collections as secondary paths.
2. **Within a collection, order is an editorial choice.** Default: proven sellers first, high-margin items in the top rows, new products slotted high enough to get a fair test (then judged on results). Never leave default sort as the silent decision-maker.
3. **Naming conventions are fixed:** one pattern per category (what it is + the distinguishing attribute), so product titles are scannable in lists, search, and ads. A clever name that hides what the product is costs clicks everywhere at once.
4. **Every product page states, in a standard order:** what it is, who it is for, the deciding specs, the objection answers (sizing, materials, shipping, returns), and proof. Structure comes from the playbook; prose comes from the writer.

## Placement hierarchy

- The homepage leads with the current merchandising plan's top pick, one seasonal/campaign story, and proven sellers — in that order. The homepage is the most expensive shelf; nothing earns a spot by being new alone.
- **Cross-sells complete the purchase** (accessories, consumables, the thing everyone buys with it); **upsells trade up before commitment** (the better version, shown at the product page, not at checkout). Checkout gets only low-consideration add-ons — nothing that reopens the decision.
- Every featured placement has an expiry date. Placements without expiry dates become furniture.

## Promotions

- Every promotion spec names: objective (move stock / acquire customers / raise order value), scope, depth, duration, stacking rules, and the margin math at expected redemption. "It's a holiday" is a calendar, not an objective.
- Check depth against each included product's price floor; exclude items that breach it rather than raising the whole promotion's cost.
- Guard the full-price business: exclude proven sellers that need no help, cap promotion frequency per category, and never let the storefront, email, and ads show different depths.

## Markdown discipline

- Slow movers (flagged by stock cover and velocity) enter a scheduled ladder: modest cut, deeper cut, final clearance — each step time-boxed. The goal is cash recovery and shelf space, so the deadest stock gets the deepest cut first.
- Record recovery per markdown (units cleared × price achieved vs. carrying the stock); the record is what calibrates the next ladder.
- A markdown is a merchandising decision, not a failure to hide: pull marked-down items out of full-price collections into a clearance path so they do not cheapen the story around them.

## Keeping channels consistent

- The merchandising plan is the single source: featured products, prices, and offers in email, social, and ads must match the storefront on the same day. Any channel that cannot honor the plan that week says so before publishing, not after.
