Storefront Ops is organized around the two halves of running an online store: getting the right people to the storefront, and making sure what they find there is worth buying and arrives without drama. The Founder & CEO sits at the top and owns the split. Demand generation is delegated wholesale to the Marketing Director, who runs a four-person growth team — content, social, lifecycle email, and paid media — against a shared monthly revenue target and a hard blended-CAC ceiling. Everything on the supply and experience side reports straight to the Founder: the Merchandising & Pricing Manager decides what the catalog says and charges, the Inventory & Supply Planner decides what gets reordered and when, the Customer Support Lead owns every customer conversation after checkout, and the Commerce Analytics Lead measures all of it.

Work moves on a weekly cadence anchored by two artifacts. The first is the trading report, produced by the Analytics Lead: sales by product and channel, margin after discounts and returns, ad efficiency, and inventory cover. The second is the merchandising plan, produced by the Merchandising Manager from that report: which products get featured, promoted, repriced, or marked down in the coming week. The Marketing Director translates the merchandising plan into channel briefs for the growth team, the Inventory Planner turns the same demand picture into purchase orders, and the Support Lead flags the products generating tickets so nobody promotes a problem. The Founder reviews both artifacts, arbitrates conflicts — usually between growth spend and margin — and signs off on anything that commits money: media budgets above the standing cap, purchase orders, and store-wide discounts.

Handoffs are written, not assumed. A campaign does not launch without a channel brief naming the products, the offer, and the margin math; a purchase order does not go out without a forecast attached; a markdown does not happen without a note on why the item slowed. When something goes wrong — a stockout mid-campaign, a promotion that sold at a loss, a support spike after a launch — the owning agent writes a short cause note into the next trading report so the same mistake costs the company only once.
