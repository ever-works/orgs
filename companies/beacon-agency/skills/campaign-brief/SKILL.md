# Campaign Brief

Run this before any creative or media work starts. The brief is the contract
between the account and the people who make the work: a thin brief produces the
wrong thing on time, which is worse than the right thing late.

## Steps

1. **State the single objective.** One measurable thing this campaign must move —
   awareness, leads, sign-ups, sales. If there are three objectives, there is no
   objective; pick the one that matters and rank the rest.
2. **Name the audience.** Who specifically, in what mindset, seeing this where.
   "Everyone" is not an audience.
3. **Set the message.** The one idea the audience should take away, and the single
   most persuasive reason to believe it. Everything downstream serves this.
4. **List the deliverables and channels.** Each asset, its channel, and its spec
   or length. Vague deliverables become scope fights later.
5. **Fix the constraints.** Brand rules, mandatory inclusions, legal or claims
   limits, tone, budget, and the deadline. Write "none stated" where true; never
   assume a constraint away.
6. **Define done.** What the finished work must satisfy to be accepted, and who
   accepts it. Make each criterion observable.
7. **Confirm before you staff.** The account manager confirms the objective and
   constraints with the client, in writing, before creative or media begins.

## Quality bar

- The objective is one sentence and measurable.
- Copy and design could each start from this brief without a follow-up call.
- Every deliverable has a channel and a spec.
- The definition of done is observable and has a named acceptor.

## Output format

    CAMPAIGN: <name>   ACCOUNT: <client>
    OBJECTIVE: <one measurable sentence>
    AUDIENCE: <who / mindset / where>
    MESSAGE: <the one idea> — <reason to believe>
    DELIVERABLES: <asset — channel — spec, each>
    CONSTRAINTS: <brand / legal / tone / budget / deadline, or "none stated">
    DONE WHEN: <observable criteria> — accepted by <name>
