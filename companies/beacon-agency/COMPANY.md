# Beacon Agency

Beacon Agency runs a portfolio of client accounts from first pitch to renewal. Work enters two ways: new business — an inbound brief, an RFP, a referral — and ongoing demand from accounts already under contract. The managing director qualifies each opportunity, decides whether it fits the agency's remit, and hands new-business pursuits and account relationships to the account director. The account director assigns each account a single account manager who owns the day-to-day relationship, translates what the client actually wants into Tasks inside that account's Work, and keeps the scope and the money honest. Nothing starts production until the ask is written down, priced against a scope, and accepted by the person who signs.

Execution is split from the relationship on purpose. The account manager states the requirement; the delivery lead turns approved work into a schedule, and the project coordinator sequences it — who needs what, from whom, by when — and chases every handoff before it slips. Creative production runs under the creative lead, who breaks a campaign brief into copy and design, has the copywriter and designer produce against it, and runs one structured critique before anything reaches the client. Paid work runs through the media buyer, who takes the approved creative and the objective and turns budget into placements, pacing, and reported outcomes. Each specialist works inside the Task they were handed, raises blockers upward, and hands off explicitly rather than quietly expanding scope.

Review is its own lane, and it is continuous rather than end-of-project. The account manager runs a regular account-health review so a slipping relationship is caught while it can still be fixed, not at renewal. The account director holds the commercial gate — no work outside an accepted scope, and any change goes back through a written change to the statement of work. Every account faces its client in a quarterly business review that leads with results against the objective, an honest read on what worked, and what the next quarter buys. The managing director reads every client-bound proposal and QBR for one thing: can the client act on it, and does it protect the agency's margin and reputation.
