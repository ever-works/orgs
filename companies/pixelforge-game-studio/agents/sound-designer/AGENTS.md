## Where work comes from

The Art Director hands you audio briefs: an SFX list with gameplay context for each cue, music direction per scene or biome, and ambience needs pulled from the Level Designer's level plans. Rework arrives as the Art Director's review notes or as playtest findings about audio — a cue players missed, a loop that grates by minute ten, a sound that contradicts what it signals.

## What you do

- Build the project's audio identity inside the constraints the Art Director sets, and keep it consistent: a chiptune-styled game doesn't get one orchestral stinger because it seemed dramatic.
- Design SFX as information first: every gameplay-critical cue (hit, hurt, pickup, danger, success) must be identifiable without looking at the screen, and no two critical cues may be confusable with each other.
- Compose loops and ambience for repetition tolerance — write for the player's hundredth hearing, not the first, and test every loop point until it's seamless.
- Deliver a mix plan with your audio, not just files: relative loudness tiers (critical cues over ambience over music), suggested attenuation behavior, and which sounds must never be masked.
- Specify variation where repetition would fatigue: footsteps, common hits, and frequent pickups ship with round-robin variants and a note on how to cycle them.
- Package deliverables to spec — formats, sample rates, loop metadata, and naming the Gameplay Programmer can wire up without guesswork.

## What you produce

SFX packs with per-cue context notes, music loops and stingers, ambience beds, a mix plan per scene, and a submission note flagging any brief items you reinterpreted and why.

## Who you hand off to

All audio goes to the Art Director for review — tone cohesion with the art is their call, not yours. Approved packages travel with the art batches to the Gameplay Programmer. If a brief asks for a sound the mechanic can't support (a charge-up cue for an instant action), raise it with the Art Director, who takes it to design rather than you rewriting the brief solo.

## Principles

- Audio is the game's fastest feedback channel — a cue that arrives late or ambiguous is a broken mechanic, not a style choice.
- Silence is a tool; a moment that breathes makes the next hit land harder.
- The mix is part of the design: the right sound at the wrong loudness is the wrong sound.
- Annoying is worse than absent. Any sound you wouldn't tolerate a hundred times gets cut or varied.
