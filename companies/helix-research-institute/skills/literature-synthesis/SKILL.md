# Literature Synthesis

Produce an evidence map organized by claim, not by source, so a reader can see at a glance where the field agrees, disagrees, and is silent.

## Procedure

1. **Frame the question.** Write the survey question in one sentence and list 3-6 sub-questions the map must answer. If you cannot, return the task for clarification.
2. **Search broadly, then narrow.** Start with reviews and meta-analyses, then primary studies, then credible practitioner or database evidence. Log every search avenue, including the ones that returned nothing.
3. **Filter to relevance.** Keep a source only if it bears on a sub-question. For each plausible-looking source you drop, record one line: source and reason.
4. **Grade what you keep** on methodology strength, scope, recency, independence, and replication. Use strong / moderate / weak with a one-line justification.
5. **Extract claims, not summaries.** Pull the specific assertions relevant to each sub-question — one claim per line, in your own words, with source and grade attached.
6. **Cluster by claim.** Put supporting and contradicting sources for the same claim together. Never average a disagreement into a mushy middle.
7. **Label consensus per claim:** established (multiple independent strong sources, no credible contradiction), contested (credible sources disagree), or unknown.
8. **Declare saturation.** Stop when new searches stop changing the map, and say so.
9. **Write the gaps section.** List what the literature cannot answer — these are candidate questions for direct measurement.

## Quality bar

- Every claim traces to a named, graded source in one hop.
- Contradictions are shown, not resolved by fiat.
- Search log and exclusion log are present — without them the map is not auditable.
- All prose is original; no quotation-stitching.

## Output format

One document: (1) question and sub-questions, (2) claim clusters with graded for/against sources and a consensus label each, (3) gaps, (4) search log, (5) exclusion log. Lead with a five-line summary of the strongest established claims and the most important contested one.
