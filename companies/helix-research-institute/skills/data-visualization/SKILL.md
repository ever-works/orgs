# Data Visualization

Choose the encoding that lets the data make its own argument — honestly, and legibly at a glance.

## Procedure

1. **Name the message.** Write the one sentence the figure should let a reader conclude. If there are two messages, there are two figures.
2. **Match chart to data and claim.** Comparison across categories → bars; trend over an ordered variable → line; distribution → histogram or box/violin; relationship → scatter; composition → stacked or part-to-whole only when parts sum meaningfully. Avoid pie charts for more than a couple of slices.
3. **Set the scale honestly.** Start bar axes at zero; never truncate to exaggerate an effect. Use log scales only when the data spans orders of magnitude, and label them as log.
4. **Show uncertainty.** Error bars, confidence bands, or visible sample points — or state plainly that uncertainty is not represented and why.
5. **Encode for accessibility.** Colorblind-safe palette, redundant encoding (shape or pattern plus color), and sufficient contrast. Do not rely on color alone to carry meaning.
6. **Reduce ink to signal.** Remove chartjunk, gridline clutter, and 3D effects. Every remaining mark should earn its place.
7. **Label completely.** Axis titles with units, legible tick labels, a title that states the message, and a caption that names the data and n.
8. **Sanity-check.** Does the visual overstate the effect? Would a skeptic accuse it of distortion? Fix that before shipping.

## Quality bar

- One figure, one message, defensible on its own.
- No truncated or misleading axes; scale choices are labeled.
- Uncertainty is shown or its absence is stated.
- Colorblind-safe and readable without relying on color alone.

## Output format

The figure plus a caption stating the message, the data source and n, and any transformation applied. Note the chart type chosen and why in one line for the writer.
