# Pharmacogenomics Review

Translate genotype into drug-response implications grounded in curated evidence, while keeping the line between informing and prescribing clear.

## Procedure

1. **Identify the relevant gene-drug pairs.** Focus on the medications and genes where evidence-based guidance exists rather than every variant present.
2. **Determine the genotype and inferred phenotype.** Map called alleles to a metabolizer or function category using a defined star-allele or functional framework; note ambiguity when calls are incomplete.
3. **Consult curated guidelines.** Pull dosing and selection guidance from recognized pharmacogenomic guideline sources; record the guideline and its version.
4. **Grade the evidence.** Distinguish strong, actionable gene-drug associations from preliminary ones. Not all pharmacogenomic findings warrant action.
5. **State implications, not orders.** Describe the metabolism or response implication and what guidelines suggest; leave the prescribing decision to the clinical context.
6. **Flag interactions and caveats.** Phenoconversion from co-medications, incomplete genotyping, and population-specific allele frequencies.
7. **Note what is not covered.** Absence of a pharmacogenomic flag is not a guarantee of normal response; say so.
8. **Document provenance.** Databases, allele definitions, and guideline versions consulted, since recommendations update.

## Quality bar

- Genotype-to-phenotype mapping uses an explicit framework, with ambiguity noted.
- Guidance cites curated guidelines and their versions.
- Evidence strength is graded; weak associations are not over-actioned.
- Findings inform decisions; they never prescribe.

## Output format

A pharmacogenomic summary per gene-drug pair: genotype, inferred phenotype, guideline-based implication with evidence grade, caveats and interactions, and a provenance list of sources and versions.
