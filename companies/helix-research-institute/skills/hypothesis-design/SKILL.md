# Hypothesis Design

Convert a fuzzy question or hunch into a hypothesis precise enough that a specific result would confirm or kill it.

## Procedure

1. **State the observation or motivation** in one sentence — what prompts this, and what decision hangs on the answer.
2. **Write the hypothesis as a claim, not a topic.** "X increases Y under condition Z" beats "we will study X and Y." Name the direction and the mechanism if one is implied.
3. **Specify variables.** Identify the independent variable(s) you manipulate or contrast, the dependent variable(s) you measure, and the covariates you must control or record.
4. **Write the null and at least one alternative** explicitly. If you cannot state a null, the hypothesis is not yet testable.
5. **Define the falsification criterion.** State the concrete result that would make you abandon the hypothesis. A hypothesis nothing could disprove is not scientific.
6. **Estimate plausibility and prior.** Note what existing evidence suggests and how surprising a positive result would be — this calibrates later interpretation.
7. **Check feasibility.** Can the needed variable actually be measured with available data or methods? If not, propose the nearest measurable proxy and name its limitations.
8. **List competing explanations** the design must rule out (confounders, reverse causation, selection).

## Quality bar

- The hypothesis is a directional, mechanistic claim, not a subject area.
- Null, alternative, and falsification criterion are all written down.
- Every named variable is measurable or has a declared proxy.
- Competing explanations are listed, not assumed away.

## Output format

A hypothesis brief: motivation, hypothesis statement, null/alternative, variables (independent / dependent / controlled), falsification criterion, prior plausibility, feasibility note, and competing explanations to exclude.
