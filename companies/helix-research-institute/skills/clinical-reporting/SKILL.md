# Clinical Reporting

Communicate clinical findings so a clinical reader can see the finding, its evidence strength, and its limits at a glance — informing, never prescribing.

## Procedure

1. **Lead with the actionable finding** and its confidence. A clinical reader should not hunt for the point.
2. **Structure predictably.** Summary, findings, evidence and methods, limitations, and references. Consistency lets a reader navigate under time pressure.
3. **Grade every finding.** Attach an evidence strength to each statement; a single case report and a randomized trial do not carry the same weight, and the report must show that.
4. **Hedge to match evidence.** Strong evidence gets direct language; uncertain findings get honest qualifiers. Never launder uncertainty into false confidence.
5. **State what is and is not actionable.** Distinguish findings that inform a decision from context that does not, and name the decision-owner as the clinician, not the report.
6. **Handle sensitive data carefully.** Include only what the report needs; note the data's provenance and handling.
7. **Make limitations prominent.** Population, sample size, confounders, and generalizability — a limitations section is mandatory, not optional.
8. **Cite sources and versions.** Every clinical claim traces to a graded source; guideline versions are recorded because they expire.

## Quality bar

- Every finding carries an explicit evidence grade.
- Language hedges in proportion to evidence.
- The report informs; the clinical decision stays with the clinician.
- Limitations and provenance are present and clear.

## Output format

A structured clinical report: summary with lead finding and confidence, graded findings, evidence and methods, a prominent limitations section, and graded references with versions.
