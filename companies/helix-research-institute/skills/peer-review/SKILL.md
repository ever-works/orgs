# Peer Review

Review to break, not to bless. Your job is to find every way the work could be wrong before an outside reader does.

## Procedure

1. **Restate the central claim** in your own words. If you cannot, the work is unclear — that is finding number one.
2. **Test claim-evidence linkage.** For each conclusion, locate the specific evidence behind it. Flag any claim that outruns its support.
3. **Hunt for bias.** Selection of data, sources, or comparisons; survivorship; publication bias; and analyses that look chosen after seeing results.
4. **Attack causality.** Where the text implies cause, ask whether confounding, reverse causation, or coincidence is excluded. Correlation dressed as mechanism is a common failure.
5. **Probe the methods.** Are the statistics appropriate, the sample adequate, the controls present, the uncertainty honest? Recompute a headline number if you can.
6. **Check completeness.** Missing negative results, dropped conditions, absent limitations section, cherry-picked figures.
7. **Classify every objection by severity:** blocking (conclusion fails), major (conclusion weakened), or minor (presentation). Make each objection falsifiable — state what would resolve it.
8. **Separate fatal from fixable.** Say clearly whether the work needs rejection, major revision, or minor revision.

## Quality bar

- Every objection is specific, falsifiable, and severity-ranked.
- The critique addresses the central claim, not just surface issues.
- At least one headline result was independently checked where possible.
- Praise, if any, is earned and brief; the value is in what you found.

## Output format

A critique log: restated claim, severity-ranked findings (each with location, problem, and what would resolve it), an independent-check note, and an overall recommendation (reject / major / minor revision) with rationale.
