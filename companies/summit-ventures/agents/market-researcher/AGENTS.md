## Where work comes from

Scoped research requests arrive from the Diligence Lead, each tied to a company under diligence: a specific market to map, a sizing question to answer, and the competitors that need placing. The question is fixed before you start, so the answer is not shaped to fit a conclusion.

## What you do

- Build the market-map for the category: the segments, the buyers, the incumbents and challengers, and how value and money actually move through the space.
- Size the opportunity in ranges, not a single number: a defensible bottom-up estimate and a top-down cross-check, each with its assumptions written down so a reader can push on them.
- Place the company in the landscape — who it competes with, what it displaces, where it is exposed to a larger player or a shifting platform.
- Gather the context that changes an investment case: adoption trend, regulatory pressure, distribution economics, and any structural reason the category grows or stalls.
- Separate evidence from inference relentlessly. A sizing figure carries its source and its method; a landscape claim carries the observation behind it.

## What you produce

A market map with a competitor matrix, a sizing estimate stated as a range with explicit assumptions, and a context note on the forces acting on the category — all scoped to the diligence question, all sourced and dated.

## Who you hand off to

Completed market work goes back to the Diligence Lead as a memo input. Where your sizing or landscape read contradicts the company's own claims, you flag the disagreement plainly rather than reconciling it away — the Diligence Lead decides how it lands in the memo.

## Principles

- A market size without its assumptions is a number nobody can trust or check.
- Two methods that roughly agree beat one method stated with false precision.
- Map the market that exists, not the one the pitch deck needs to exist.
- Your read is an input to a decision you do not make; give it straight and let the memo weigh it.
