# Diligence Memo

The firm's single diligence format, used to write memos on the diligence desk and to read them at the decision gate. A memo answers whether the evidence supports pursuing a company, shows its work, and cannot be mistaken for an instruction to invest.

## Structure

1. **Header block** — company, the investment question, requesting Task, author, date, and status (draft / gate-ready / decided).
2. **Recommendation framing** — at most five sentences: pursue-with-conditions or pass, the confidence level, and the single most important reason. Framed as analysis; never phrased as an order to invest.
3. **Company** — what it does, stage, team, product, and the traction the evidence actually supports — separated from what the pitch claims.
4. **Evidence table** — one row per material finding: the finding, its source (document, data, call, or the market-map appendix), the source date, and a confidence grade (high / moderate / low). No row, no claim in the prose.
5. **Market** — the sized opportunity as a range with its assumptions, and the competitive placement, drawn from the market-map input.
6. **Scenario analysis** — bull, base, and bear cases, each with named assumptions and a rough likelihood. The bear case gets the same depth as the bull, measured in assumptions examined.
7. **Risk register** — every material risk, severity-ranked, each with whether it is resolvable, mitigated, or open at the deadline.
8. **Conditions** — for a pursue framing, the specific things that would have to be true or verified next; for a pass, what would have to change to reopen it.
9. **Sources** — every document, dataset, and call cited, with dates, so the memo can be audited without its author.
10. **Disclaimer block** — verbatim: this document is internal analysis for the firm's own decision-making only; it is not financial, investment, legal, or tax advice; it makes no recommendation to any person to buy, sell, or hold any instrument; the firm executes no transactions.

## Quality bar

- The memo states plainly what would make it a pass — a reader can see the kill conditions.
- Every number in the prose traces to an evidence-table row or the market appendix in one hop.
- Confidence language travels with each claim, never pooled into a general caveat.
- The bear case is argued, not gestured at.
- No sentence reads as an instruction to invest, including in the recommendation framing.

## Output format

A single memo following the structure above, plus the market-map appendix it draws on and a change log once it reaches gate-ready. The status line at top makes draft versus decided unmistakable.
