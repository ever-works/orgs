# Portfolio Review

The firm's standing cadence for keeping invested companies tracked and supported. It rates direction and concentrates attention — it is operations and analysis, never advice to buy, sell, or move money.

## Steps

1. **Set the cadence and scope.** Confirm which companies are in this review and the period it covers. Every company gets touched on schedule; none is skipped because it is quiet.
2. **Pull the numbers.** For each company, gather the current KPIs and the latest check-in notes. Where a number is missing or stale, mark it missing — do not carry the last known figure forward as if it were current.
3. **Compare to last review.** Rate each KPI's movement against the prior cycle: improving, flat, or deteriorating. Health is the trend, not the snapshot.
4. **Rate health with a reason.** Give each company a status (healthy / watch / at-risk) and one sentence of why, tied to the movement — not just a color.
5. **Rank attention.** Order the portfolio by where the firm's time changes an outcome. Name plainly which companies are coasting, which need a touch, and which are at risk.
6. **Turn needs into tracked work.** Every support commitment — a hire, an intro, a strategic question — becomes a scoped internal Task with an owner and a due date, tracked to a result, not a promise.
7. **Escalate early.** Any company trending toward trouble goes up with its trend and the options, before it becomes a surprise.

## Quality bar

- Every health rating cites the KPI movement behind it.
- Missing or stale data is flagged as such, never silently forward-filled.
- The attention ranking reflects impact, not company size or loudness.
- Every support commitment maps to a tracked Task with an owner and a date.
- The review stewards and analyzes; it never advises anyone to buy, sell, or move money.

## Output format

A portfolio health review: per-company status with KPI movement and a one-line reason, a ranked attention list, a support-action table (Task, owner, due, state), and an escalation section for at-risk companies.
