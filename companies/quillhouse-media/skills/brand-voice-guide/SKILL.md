# Brand Voice Guide

Three formats, one publisher. The voice is what a reader recognizes before they see the logo. This is the standard that briefs cite and copy editing enforces.

## Steps

1. **State the voice in adjectives with edges.** Pick a few traits and, for each, the trait it is not — "plainspoken, not flat," "confident, not glib." A trait with no opposite is decoration, not a rule.
2. **Anchor with paired examples.** For each trait, write a short on-voice line and the off-voice version beside it. Editors settle far more disputes from examples than from adjectives.
3. **Set the mechanics.** Decide the recurring calls — person and tense, contractions, sentence rhythm, how numbers and titles are written, the words the house prefers and the ones it avoids — so consistency is a lookup, not a debate.
4. **Map the voice across formats.** Name how the one voice bends per surface: the book more measured, the newsletter more direct, the podcast more spoken — same identity, different register — so "on-voice" is defined for each.
5. **Apply it while drafting.** Writers write to the guide, not to their own defaults; when a brief and the guide disagree, the disagreement is raised before drafting, not smoothed over after.
6. **Enforce it in copy edit.** The copy editor flattens off-voice lines toward the guide and escalates only the genuine questions the guide does not settle — new territory, not restatements of a settled call.
7. **Keep it current.** When a settled escalation creates a new rule, write it into the guide with its example so the next piece inherits the decision instead of relitigating it.

## Quality bar

- Every trait carries its opposite and a paired on/off-voice example.
- Mechanics are decided, not left to each writer's habit.
- The voice's register is defined for all three formats.
- Escalations that set a precedent are written back into the guide.

## Output format

A living voice guide: trait-and-opposite pairs with examples, a mechanics reference, per-format register notes, and a short changelog of precedent-setting decisions folded back in.
