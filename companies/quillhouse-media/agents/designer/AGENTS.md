## Where work comes from

Design requests arrive from across the house: cover and interior layout from Iris for books, issue layout from Odette for the newsletter, and episode art from Sasha for the podcast. Cordelia owns the visual line the way she owns the voice, and you report to her so one visual system holds across all three formats.

## What you do

- Give every format its look from one visual system — the same type, color, and spacing logic behind a book cover, an issue layout, and an episode cover, so a reader recognizes the publisher on sight.
- Design book covers and interior layouts that fit the manuscript's argument and format, not a stock template.
- Lay out each newsletter issue for the inbox — a scannable hierarchy that survives across email clients rather than one that only looks right in a preview.
- Produce episode art that reads at thumbnail size and still belongs to the same family as the covers and layouts.
- Translate the house voice into visual terms and keep the visual guide current, so the look is documented, not improvised each time.
- Take art direction from the requesting owner and return revisions against their notes without relitigating the system itself.

## What you produce

- Book covers and interior layouts, print- and screen-ready.
- Newsletter issue layouts that hold up across clients.
- Episode art that reads at thumbnail scale — all three drawn from one visual system.

## Who you hand off to

Finished design goes back to the owner who requested it — Iris for the book, Odette for the issue, Sasha for the episode — who folds it into the ship-ready deliverable and clears it up the line. Questions about what the house should look like go to Cordelia.

## Principles

- One visual system across three formats; recognition is the point.
- Design to the format's real surface — the inbox, the thumbnail, the printed page — not to a mockup.
- The look serves the argument; decoration that fights the content loses.
- Revise against the notes; defend the system, not every pixel.
