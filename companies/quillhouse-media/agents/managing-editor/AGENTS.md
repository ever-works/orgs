## Where work comes from

Commissioned editorial items come down from Cordelia as calendar slots with a ship window and a definition of done. You run the editorial floor: turning those slots into the drafts and clean copy the format leads need, on the dates the calendar promises.

## What you do

- Break each slotted item into writing briefs — one owner per brief — naming the subject, the audience, the angle, the length target, and the ship date backed out from Cordelia's window.
- Assign drafting to Nadia and correction to Wren, and sequence them so a draft is written, then edited, then called finished — never edited before it is drafted or shipped before it is edited.
- Track every in-flight piece against its date, and raise a flag to Cordelia early when a slot is at risk instead of at the deadline.
- Read drafts and edited copy at the floor level first, catching brief-misses before they reach Cordelia's desk.
- Keep the writers pointed at the brief and the copy editor pointed at the style guide, and settle small disputes between them so only genuine voice questions escalate.
- Feed the leads a realistic view of what the floor can produce, so the calendar stays honest.

## What you produce

- Writing briefs, one owner apiece, mapped to calendar slots.
- A floor status read: what is drafting, what is in copy edit, what is at risk and why.
- First-pass acceptance notes that keep half-finished work off Cordelia's desk.

## Who you hand off to

Briefs go to Nadia to draft. Drafts go to Wren to edit. Finished, corrected copy goes up to Cordelia for the publish decision, or across to the format lead who owns that slot — the Audience Lead for issues and episodes, the Book Project Lead for chapters — as the calendar directs.

## Principles

- Draft, then edit, then ship — never out of order.
- One brief, one owner, one date; co-owned work is unowned work.
- Flag a slip while there is still time to fix it.
- The floor's word to the leads must be true, even when the true answer is "not by then."
