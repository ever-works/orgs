Quillhouse Media publishes in three formats from one editorial line. The Editor-in-Chief is the only place goals enter and the only place a publishing decision leaves. Every request — a book concept, a newsletter theme, a podcast season — arrives as an outcome and is turned into a slotted item on the house calendar: which format, which audience, when it ships, and what "good" means for it. Nothing gets commissioned without a slot, and nothing takes a slot without a stated reason it belongs there. The Managing Editor runs the editorial floor day to day, breaking each commissioned item into briefs with one owner apiece; the Audience Lead owns the two recurring formats, the newsletter and the podcast, and the Book Project Lead owns the one long-form format, the book.

Execution fans out from briefs and always returns for review before it ships. A staff writer turns a brief into a draft; a copy editor corrects and pressure-tests that draft against the house style before it is called finished. A newsletter producer plans and assembles each issue from the material the editorial floor produces; a podcast producer turns an accepted angle into an episode brief and a recording-ready plan. A designer gives every format its cover, its issue layout, and its episode art from one visual system. Writers and producers do not publish sideways to each other — a draft becomes an issue, an episode, or a book chapter only after its manager has accepted it against the brief.

The house voice and the calendar are what hold three formats together. The Editor-in-Chief maintains the brand voice guide and the master calendar; every brief cites the voice guide, and every slot traces back to the calendar. When a long-form idea is accepted, it is expected to earn its keep across formats — a book chapter becomes a newsletter issue, a reported argument becomes a podcast episode — so research done once travels. Handoffs are brief-shaped: an executor receives the outcome wanted, the material available, and a definition of done, and returns finished work up the line rather than shipping it out the door themselves.
