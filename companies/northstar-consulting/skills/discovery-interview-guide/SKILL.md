# Discovery Interview Guide

A playbook for getting decision-grade evidence out of user conversations. The failure mode it prevents: sessions that collect opinions about a hypothetical future instead of facts about the recent past.

## Plan the study (before any session)

1. Write the research question at the top of the guide. One question per study; a second question means a second study or an explicit trade-off.
2. Define the participant profile: the behavior that qualifies someone (e.g. "canceled in the last 90 days"), not the demographic. Recruit 5-8; note anyone who slips the profile.
3. Draft the question arc in four blocks:
   - **Context** (5 min): their role, their workflow, where the product sits in it.
   - **Recent-past walkthrough** (20 min): "Walk me through the last time you…" — the core of the session. Follow the specific instance, not the general habit.
   - **Friction probes** (10 min): "What happened next?", "What did that cost you?", "What did you try instead?"
   - **Wrap** (5 min): "What should I have asked about?"
4. Strike every question that pitches, leads, or asks the participant to predict their own behavior ("Would you use…?" is banned).

## Run the session

5. Open by stating you are there to learn, not to sell, and that criticism is the most useful thing they can give.
6. Ask about the last specific occurrence, then go silent. Count to five before rescuing a pause.
7. When they generalize ("I usually…"), pull them back: "Tell me about the most recent time."
8. Capture verbatims with their trigger — record the question that prompted each quote.
9. If they request a feature, dig for the underlying situation twice ("What would that let you do? And what does that get you?") before writing it down.

## Code the evidence (within 24 hours of the last session)

10. Cluster observations into patterns; a pattern needs at least two independent participants.
11. Count: for each pattern, note how many participants exhibited it versus merely agreed with it when prompted.
12. Separate said-vs-did explicitly — two columns, never merged.
13. Write the confidence note: sample weaknesses, questions that turned out to be leading, segments missing.

## Quality bar

- Every claim traceable to at least one dated verbatim.
- Null results reported ("no participant raised X unprompted").
- The packet answers the research question it was assigned — or opens by saying it could not.

## Output format

Evidence packet: research question → method and participant table → patterned findings with counts → supporting verbatims → confidence note.
