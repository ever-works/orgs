# Competitor Gap Analysis

The output is a short list of winnable openings — topics where demand exists and the current best answer is beatable — not an exhaustive audit of everyone else's site.

## Steps

1. **Fix the field.** Name three to five real competitors: the ones who actually rank for the queries you want, not the ones you compare yourself to in pitches. Re-derive this list from search results, because the competitor for a query is whoever ranks for it.
2. **Map their coverage.** For each competitor, list the topic clusters they publish in and mark each cluster: strong (thorough, current, well-structured), weak (thin, outdated, or off-intent), or absent. A spreadsheet with clusters as rows and competitors as columns is enough.
3. **Overlay demand.** Cross the map with your own demand signals — opportunity queries from search performance data, rising topics from trend scouting. A gap with no demand behind it is not an opening; it is a topic nobody covers because nobody cares.
4. **Classify each opening.** Three kinds, in rising order of difficulty:
   - **Unclaimed** — real demand, no competitor has a dedicated answer. Fastest win.
   - **Underserved** — competitors cover it thinly or stale. Win by being visibly more complete or more current; name exactly what "more" means.
   - **Contested** — everyone covers it well. Enter only with a genuinely different angle or asset; "the same but ours" loses here.
5. **Write the verdict line.** Each surviving opening gets one line: the topic, the class, the competitor evidence, and what a winning piece must contain that the field lacks. This line is what the content brief is built from.

## Quality bar

- Every opening cites the demand signal AND the competitor evidence; either alone is not enough.
- The list is ranked and short — five openings with verdict lines beat forty raw gaps.
- Contested entries state the differentiated angle explicitly or they are cut.
- Re-run the analysis on a cadence; a gap map older than a quarter is fiction.

## Output format

A ranked openings list: topic, class (unclaimed / underserved / contested), demand evidence, competitor evidence, and the one-line requirement for a winning piece.
