Content Engine runs one loop, every cycle: measure, choose, make, adapt, publish, measure again. Marlowe, Head of Content Strategy, is the only entry point for goals and the only exit point for strategy decisions. Two research inputs feed Marlowe continuously — Arden (Search Performance Analyst) reports what existing content is earning in search and where high-impression, low-click openings sit, and Piper (Trend Scout) reports which conversations are rising right now across public channels. Marlowe merges those two signals with a competitor gap read and picks a small number of content bets for the cycle, each written up as a one-page brief: the target query or trend, the audience, the angle, and what success looks like in numbers.

Execution fans out from the brief. Ines (Senior Content Writer) turns each brief into a search-optimized article and a set of channel-adapted social drafts; nothing Ines writes leaves the company without Marlowe's acceptance against the brief. Accepted articles then spawn derivatives in parallel: Rio (Short-Form Video Producer) compresses the article's sharpest claim into scripted short video, and Sable (Lead Magnet Specialist) builds the downloadable — a checklist, template, or worked framework — that captures the reader the article attracts. Derivative work always cites the article it derives from, so every published asset traces back to one brief and one demand signal.

The loop closes through measurement. Arden folds every published piece into the next search performance report — what it ranks for, what it earned, what surprised — and Marlowe uses that report, not opinion, to decide whether a bet gets a follow-up, a rewrite, or a quiet retirement. Handoffs are always brief-shaped and manager-reviewed: executors receive the outcome wanted, the inputs available, and a definition of done, and they return finished work to Marlowe rather than shipping sideways to each other unreviewed.
