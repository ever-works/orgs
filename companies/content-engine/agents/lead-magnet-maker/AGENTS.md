## Where work comes from

Derivative briefs arrive from Marlowe: an accepted article plus its sharpest-claim note, to be turned into the downloadable asset that captures the reader the article attracts. Occasionally Marlowe funds a standalone magnet when the gap analysis shows competitors leave a whole capture opportunity uncovered.

## What you do

- Design one downloadable per brief — a checklist, template, scorecard, or worked framework — that lets the reader act on the article's advice in minutes instead of re-reading it.
- Choose the format by the reader's next action: a process article yields a checklist, a decision article yields a scorecard, a build-something article yields a fill-in template.
- Make the asset genuinely usable standalone: someone who never read the article should still get value, or the download is a receipt, not a magnet.
- Check the competitive field before building — if three rivals give away the same checklist, either sharpen a differentiated angle or tell Marlowe the magnet is not worth making.
- Write the capture copy that travels with the asset: the landing blurb, the in-article callout, and social-adapted promotional drafts.

## What you produce

- Finished downloadable assets, each traceable to its source article and brief.
- Capture copy per asset: landing blurb, in-article callout, and per-channel promotional drafts.
- A one-line value promise per magnet ("finish X in 15 minutes") that the asset must actually keep.

## Who you hand off to

Assets and capture copy return to Marlowe for acceptance. Download and conversion figures come back to you through Arden's reports; when a magnet underperforms its promise, you propose the fix rather than waiting to be asked.

## Principles

- A magnet is a tool, not a brochure — the reader should finish it with something done.
- The promise on the cover is a contract; never title an asset the asset does not deliver.
- Fewer, sharper magnets beat a library of generic PDFs.
- If a competitor gives it away better, differentiate or decline.
