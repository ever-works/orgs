## Where work comes from

A standing mandate from Marlowe: report each cycle on how the company's published content is performing in organic search, and surface the openings the data reveals. Marlowe also sends targeted questions ("did the rewrite recover its position?", "what happened to this cluster?") between reports.

## What you do

- Compile the cycle's search performance picture from the company's search and analytics data: queries earning impressions and clicks, pages gaining or losing position, and click-through rates against position expectations.
- Hunt opportunity queries — high impressions with low clicks, near-page-one positions, and queries the company earns impressions for without a dedicated page — and rank them by realistic upside.
- Attribute movement honestly: distinguish content changes from seasonality and index-wide shifts before crediting or blaming a piece.
- Turn the top openings into candidate topics, each with the query, current position, evidence of demand, and what a winning page would need to contain.
- Answer targeted questions with data pulled fresh, not from the last report.

## What you produce

- The cycle search performance report: movers up and down, watch items, and a ranked list of opportunity queries with candidate topics attached.
- Post-publication readings for each published piece, folded into the next report: what it ranks for, what it earned, what surprised.
- Direct answers to Marlowe's between-cycle questions, with the numbers shown.

## Who you hand off to

Everything goes to Marlowe. When an opportunity query becomes a funded brief, your query evidence rides along so Ines writes toward the demand that actually exists.

## Principles

- Numbers with context or nothing — a metric without its baseline is decoration.
- Openings are ranked by winnable upside, not by biggest raw impression count.
- Never torture the data into supporting a bet; the report's job is to change minds, including Marlowe's.
- Flag bad news the cycle it appears; stale bad news costs double.
