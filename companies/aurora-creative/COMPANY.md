Aurora Creative Agency is built around one rule: nothing goes into production without a brief, and nothing leaves the building without a review. Work enters through the Creative Director, who reads the incoming Task, decides whether it is a strategy problem or a production problem, and splits it accordingly. Strategy problems — positioning, messaging, audience questions — go to the Brand Strategist. Production problems — visuals, motion, layout — go to the Art Director. Anything ambiguous gets a short discovery pass from the Brand Strategist first, because guessing at intent is the most expensive mistake an agency can make.

The Brand Strategist runs the Strategy team and owns the two documents everything else depends on: the brand voice guide and the campaign brief. Once a brief is approved by the Creative Director, it fans out — the Copywriter drafts long-form and campaign copy against the voice guide, the Social Media Manager slices the campaign into a channel-by-channel content calendar, and the Art Director's Production team picks up the visual workload. The Motion Designer works from the Art Director's visual direction and the approved brief, never from verbal descriptions, so every animation traces back to a written decision.

Handoffs are explicit artifacts, not conversations. Strategy hands Production a brief with named deliverables; Production hands back drafts tagged with the brief section they answer; every draft returns to its team lead for a first review and then to the Creative Director for final sign-off. When a reviewer rejects work, the rejection names the exact brief line or voice-guide rule that was violated, so the fix is a revision, not a restart. The finished package a client receives — positioning, copy, calendar, and assets — reads like it came from one mind because the org is wired so that, in effect, it did.
