# Campaign Brief

The brief is the contract between strategy and production. Every deliverable in the campaign must trace to a section of it; anything that can't is scope creep wearing a costume.

## Writing the brief

1. **Objective.** One sentence: the business outcome this campaign exists to cause, stated so you could later say whether it happened. "Raise awareness" fails this test; "get trial signups from mid-size teams" passes.
2. **Audience.** Who exactly, what they currently believe, and what they should believe after the campaign. Three lines, written in plain language a stranger could act on.
3. **Single-minded message.** ONE sentence the audience should take away. If you need "and", you have two campaigns — split them. Everything downstream is judged against this line.
4. **Support.** The two or three reasons the message is credible: proof points, product facts, or claims the client has authorized. Mark anything unverified as an assumption.
5. **Deliverables list.** Every asset the campaign requires: name, format, channel, owner role, and due order. This list defines "campaign done".
6. **Constraints.** Budget, deadline, mandatory elements (logos, legal lines, claims to avoid), and channel restrictions. Unstated constraints surface as rejections later — hunt them now.
7. **Success measure.** How the objective will be checked, even roughly, and when.

## Review pass before sign-off

- Read only the single-minded message and the deliverables list: could a stranger produce roughly the right campaign? If not, the middle sections are decoration.
- Check each deliverable against the objective — cut any deliverable that exists because it's traditional rather than because it serves the objective.
- Confirm every claim in Support has a source or an assumption flag.

## Quality bar

- One page. A brief nobody reads protects nobody.
- Zero adjectives doing load-bearing work — "bold, fresh look" is a mood, not an instruction.
- The brief is versioned; changes after sign-off get a new version and a one-line changelog, so production always knows which brief is live.

## Output format

Markdown document with the seven numbered sections above, a version/date line at the top, and the approver's sign-off line at the bottom.
