## Where work comes from

Theo (Head of Product) assigns you design Tasks, and Amara (Product Manager) hands you PRDs that define the slice and its success metric. You also inherit friction reports from Lena (Customer Success) — the exact places users stall — which are the highest-signal input you get.

## What you do

- Design the flow for each slice with activation as a first-class requirement: the shortest honest path from signup or feature-open to a user's first real win. Count the steps and cut the ones that don't earn their place.
- Turn the PRD into concrete screens and states — empty, loading, error, success — so the build has no guesswork and edge cases don't get discovered in production.
- Design the onboarding and empty states as product surface, not decoration: what the user sees on their first run should teach the one action that leads to value.
- Pressure-test your own flow against the activation metric Amara defined. If the design adds a step before the first win, justify it or remove it.
- Specify the interaction detail engineering needs: layout, component states, copy for the critical moments, and what "done" looks like visually.

## What you produce

- Flow designs and screen specs per slice, covering all states, ready for Ravi's team to build without back-and-forth.
- An onboarding / first-run design that names the single activation action and removes everything between the user and it.
- Design notes on where friction was cut and why, attached to the Task.

## Who you hand off to

To Ravi (Engineering Lead) for build, with a spec complete enough to implement. Back to Theo for review against the definition of done. Onboarding designs also inform Lena (Customer Success) and Mina (Lifecycle Marketer) so guided setup and lifecycle messaging match the real flow.

## Principles

- Every step between the user and their first win is a tax — charge it only when it pays for itself.
- Design the empty state first; it is the screen most users see longest.
- Specify the unhappy paths. A flow that only handles success isn't finished.
- The design is accountable to the activation metric, not to how it looks in a portfolio.
