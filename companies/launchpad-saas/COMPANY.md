Launchpad SaaS exists to take a startup from "we think teams have this problem" to "teams sign up, activate themselves, and pay to keep using it." Every goal enters through the CEO, who reframes it as a bet with a clear outcome and a single owner, then routes it to one of four direct reports: the Head of Product for anything build-or-learn, the Engineering Lead for anything about how fast and safely the product can change, the Growth Lead for reach and self-serve conversion, and the Sales Lead for direct revenue and retention. The CEO writes the mandate — the outcome, why now, and what good looks like — and deliberately leaves the how to the person who owns it.

Inside the Product team, the Head of Product decomposes each mandate into scoped Tasks with an explicit definition of done. The Product Manager writes the PRD that turns a fuzzy bet into a testable slice, the UX Designer designs the flow with activation built in rather than bolted on, and the Engineering Lead pressure-tests feasibility and sequences the build. Nothing is called "done" until the person who owns the Task has reviewed it against its definition of done — the manager who scoped the work is the one who accepts or returns it. Growth and Sales run in parallel: the Growth Lead builds the loops that bring users in and move them to their first win, while the Sales Lead and Customer Success Lead convert and retain the accounts that need a human.

Work moves as updates on Tasks so the chain from bet to shipped feature to activated account stays traceable. A shipped feature hands off from Product to Growth (so the loop that exposes it to users gets built) and to Customer Success (so onboarding reflects what changed). Signals flow back the other way: what activation data and sales calls reveal becomes the next round of discovery. The CEO holds the roadmap in Now / Next / Later, re-ranks every cycle against what the loop is actually returning, and owns the final call whenever two teams want the same scarce week of engineering time.
