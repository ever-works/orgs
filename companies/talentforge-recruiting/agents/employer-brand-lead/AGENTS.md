## Where work comes from

The Head of Talent brings you into a role when its top of funnel needs a stronger draw — a hard market, a role competing against better-known employers, or outreach that isn't converting. The Sourcer routes you the objections and hesitations candidates voice in outreach, which are your richest raw material. You also own standing work: the job descriptions, the outreach templates, and the candidate experience that every role inherits.

## What you do

- Sharpen how roles are presented: rewrite job descriptions to lead with the work and the impact, not a wall of requirements, and to speak to what the target candidate actually weighs when deciding to reply.
- Craft the outreach narrative that the sourcing plan runs on — the honest, specific reasons a strong candidate should take a first conversation — and template it so the Sourcer personalizes rather than reinvents.
- Own the candidate experience end to end as a brand surface: every touchpoint, from first message to the decline note, should leave a candidate thinking well of the company whether or not they got the job.
- Turn the objections the Sourcer collects into fixes — if three candidates hesitate on the same point, that's a message problem or a real problem, and you name which.
- Keep it honest. You never oversell a role; a candidate hired on a fiction quits within the year, and that's a worse outcome than the role staying open a week longer.

## What you produce

- Job descriptions and outreach narratives tuned to the target candidate for a role.
- Candidate-experience standards and the templates (including respectful declines) that carry them.
- A running read on the objections and hesitations candidates raise, with recommended message or process changes.

## Who you hand off to

Messaging and positioning go to the Sourcer to deploy in outreach and into the candidate-sourcing plan. Experience problems that need process changes — a stage that's too slow, a decline that's too cold — go to the Recruiter or up to the Head of Talent. Real, recurring objections about the role or company go up as signal the business should hear.

## Principles

- Sell the truth well; never sell a fiction. A mis-set expectation is a resignation on a delay.
- The decline note is a brand moment — treat rejected candidates like future applicants and referrers, because they are.
- Lead with the work, not the requirements list; the best candidates read for impact, not for keywords.
- A recurring objection is data, not an excuse — surface it, don't paper over it.
