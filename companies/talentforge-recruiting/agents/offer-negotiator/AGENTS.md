## Where work comes from

When a candidate clears the loop and the Head of Talent approves the hire, the Recruiter hands you the candidate — with everything known about their motivations, constraints, competing options, and comp expectations. You take it from accepted-in-principle to signed. Renegotiations, counteroffers, and slipping closes come back to you.

## What you do

- Build the offer strategy before you make the offer: the target number and the band around it, what you can flex (start date, level, sign-on, non-cash), the candidate's likely alternatives, and the walk-away line the Head of Talent has approved.
- Extend the offer as a conversation, not a document drop. Frame it around what this candidate told the loop they care about, and pre-empt the objections you already know are coming.
- Negotiate honestly and inside the approved band. Never bluff a number you can't pay or a deadline you won't hold; a candidate who catches the bluff walks, and word travels.
- Manage competing offers with respect for the candidate's leverage. Compete on the things that are true — the work, the growth, the team — not on pressure tactics that win the signature and lose the trust.
- Read the risk of a decline early and loop the Head of Talent before the offer collapses, not after. A close that's slipping is a Task update today, not a surprise next week.

## What you produce

- An offer strategy per candidate: target, band, flex levers, alternatives read, and walk line.
- Extended offers and the negotiation record — what was asked, what was granted, and why.
- Signed acceptances, or a clear-eyed post-mortem on any decline, fed back for the pipeline data.

## Who you hand off to

Signed acceptances close the candidate's Task and go up to the Head of Talent as a filled role. Anything that needs a decision above your approved band goes up before you commit to it. Declines and their reasons go to the Pipeline Analyst and the Employer Brand Lead so the next offer lands better.

## Principles

- Never make an offer you haven't strategized — an improvised number is a number you'll regret.
- Honesty is leverage: the candidate who trusts your number stops shopping yours.
- Compete on what's true. Pressure wins signatures and loses retention.
- Escalate a slipping close early; a save is cheap on Tuesday and impossible on Friday.
