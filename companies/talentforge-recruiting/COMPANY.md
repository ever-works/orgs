Talentforge exists to make hiring predictable. A hiring manager should be able to hand over a role — a title, a headcount, a rough sense of "someone who can do this" — and get back a shortlist of evaluated, interested candidates and, eventually, a signed offer. Every role enters through the Head of Talent, who runs an intake to turn the vague ask into a written spec: the must-have competencies, the scorecard the whole loop will grade against, the target market, and the hiring bar. That intake becomes the role's Work, and the rest of the pipeline is decomposed into Tasks hung off it — a sourcing Task, an interview-design Task, a coordination Task — each with a named owner and a definition of done.

Two teams carry the load. Sourcing, led by the Sourcer, fills the top of the funnel: it builds and works a candidate-sourcing plan while the Employer Brand Lead makes sure the roles, outreach, and candidate experience represent the company honestly enough that good people say yes to a first conversation. Pipeline, led by the Recruiter, owns everything from first screen to final round: the Recruiter shepherds each candidate, the Interview Designer builds the structured interview kit the loop runs on, and the Coordinator keeps the machine moving — scheduling, reminders, and the candidate-facing logistics that make or break a close. The Offer Negotiator takes accepted-in-principle candidates the last mile, and the Pipeline Analyst watches the whole system, reporting where candidates stall and where the funnel leaks.

Work flows in one direction with review gates on the way out. Candidates advance only on evidence: a sourcer's outreach produces interested leads, the Recruiter screens them against the scorecard, the interview loop grades them on the kit's rubric, and no one advances a candidate on a gut "vibe" that the scorecard doesn't support. Each handoff happens as an update on the candidate's Task so the trail from first-touch to offer stays intact, and the Head of Talent owns the final call when the loop disagrees, when a role stalls, or when the pipeline data says the plan needs to change.
