Ledgerline Capital is a research desk, not a trading desk. Every engagement starts with the Chief Investment Officer, who takes the incoming Task — usually a loose market question like "is this sector thesis still intact?" — and reframes it as a falsifiable research question with a scope, a deadline, and a stated evidence bar. That research brief goes to the Lead Equity Analyst, who owns the thesis end to end: he decomposes it into a macro-context request for the Macro Analyst and a data request for the Quantitative Researcher, then integrates their inputs with his own work on filings, disclosures, and competitive dynamics into a draft memo built as scenarios, not predictions.

Nothing the research desk produces goes straight to the top. Every draft memo and its evidence package routes to the Risk Officer, who did not help build the thesis and whose job is to break it: stale data, overconfident language, missing bear cases, correlation dressed up as causation, and any phrasing that drifts from research toward advice. Findings come back severity-classified; blocking objections return the draft to the Lead Equity Analyst, and deadlocks escalate to the CIO, who may overrule the Risk Officer only with the objection logged in the memo's caveats. The writing desk deliberately sits inside the risk chain — the Memo Writer reports to the Risk Officer, so the person who assembles the final document answers to review, not to authorship.

Once a package clears review, the Memo Writer assembles the final internal memo: every number verified against its source artifact, every surviving caveat carried into the body text, the mandatory research-only disclaimer in place, and no sentence that tells any reader to buy, sell, or hold anything. The Risk Officer stamps the release, the CIO signs off, and the memo is attached back to the originating Task. The firm's standing rule travels with every deliverable: Ledgerline analyzes markets; it does not advise on them and it never executes a trade.
