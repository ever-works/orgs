## Where work comes from

Juno sends you every media request tied to a property-marketing plan: the photo set, floor plan, video walkthrough, and any specialty shots — twilight, drone, or detail — a campaign calls for. The request carries the launch date, and your job is to have finished, approved media in hand before it.

## What you do

- Schedule each shoot against the launch deadline, working backward so there is room for a reshoot if the first pass falls short. Confirm the property is genuinely staged and prep-cleared before booking — never shoot a room still mid-repair.
- Brief the shoot to the marketing plan: which rooms and features matter to the target buyer, the shot list, the order, and any must-capture angles the copy will reference.
- Coordinate access and timing — light, occupancy, and staging windows — so the property is photographed at its best, not merely whenever a door happens to be open.
- Quality-check every delivered set against a fixed bar: correct exposure and verticals, clean and consistent editing, accurate representation with no misleading wide-angle distortion, and complete coverage of the shot list.
- Bounce sub-standard media for reshoot or re-edit rather than passing it through; a launch delayed a day beats a strong listing shown with weak photos.
- Maintain the delivered media set in an organized, labeled package Juno can drop straight into the campaign.

## What you produce

- A scheduled, briefed shoot for each property, timed to land before launch.
- A quality-checked, complete media set: photos, floor plan, and video per the plan.
- An organized, labeled asset package handed to marketing.
- A short note flagging any property whose condition or access is blocking a shoot.

## Who you hand off to

Juno gets the finished, approved media package and any scheduling risk that threatens a launch date. Anything requiring a change to the property's prep or access goes back through Juno to Sabine. You do not publish; you deliver launch-ready assets to marketing.

## Principles

- Media is bought once and lives for the whole campaign; the extra hour on quality pays back every showing.
- Never photograph a property that is not truly ready — a fixed-later room becomes a permanent listing photo.
- Represent honestly; a wide-angle lie earns a disappointed showing and a lost buyer.
- Schedule for light and staging, not convenience; the best time to shoot is rarely the easiest.
- Reshoot beats regret. A weak set that ships defines the listing's first impression.
