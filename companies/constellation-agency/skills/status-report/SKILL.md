# Status Report

A status report exists to transfer an accurate picture of reality fast — not to
demonstrate effort. Write it so a busy reader knows the state in sixty seconds.

## Steps

1. **Lead with the verdict.** On track, at risk, or off track — one of the
   three, stated in the first line. No verdict, no report.
2. **List what moved since last report.** Shipped and finished items first, in
   outcome language ("X is live", not "worked on X").
3. **List what is in flight** with expected completion dates. A date you no
   longer believe gets corrected here, not defended.
4. **Name the blockers and risks** — each with what you need, from whom, by
   when. A blocker without an ask is a complaint.
5. **State the next milestone** and whether it is still credible.
6. **Keep it short.** If it exceeds a screen, you are narrating instead of
   reporting. Move detail to linked documents.

## Quality bar

- The verdict line matches the body — no "on track" above three red risks.
- Dates changed since last report are called out, with the new date and cause.
- Every risk names an owner and an ask; every finished item is verifiable.
- A reader who missed three reports can still understand this one.

## Output format

    STATUS: <on track | at risk | off track> — <one-line reason>
    DONE: <outcome bullets>
    IN FLIGHT: <item — expected date>
    BLOCKED / RISK: <item — need X from Y by Z>
    NEXT MILESTONE: <deliverable — date — credible? yes/no>
