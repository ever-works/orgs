# Retro

A retro succeeds when something the team does actually changes. It fails when
it produces a warm document nobody reads. Optimize for the former.

## Steps

1. **Gather the facts first.** Timeline, plan-versus-actual, and the numbers.
   Facts before feelings prevents the retro from becoming a mood.
2. **Collect input from everyone involved** — asynchronously and before the
   discussion, so the loudest voice does not set the frame.
3. **Sort observations into three lists.** Went well (keep doing), went badly
   (change), and got lucky (went well but would fail again — treat as risk).
4. **Find causes, not culprits.** For each "went badly", ask why until you reach
   a process cause. A retro that names people produces silence next time.
5. **Choose at most three changes.** Each with a named owner and a check date.
   Ten actions is zero actions wearing a list.
6. **File the lessons** with the knowledge base so the next team can find them —
   dated, searchable, and honest about what was luck.
7. **Check the previous retro's actions** at the start of this one. Unfinished
   actions from last time are this retro's first agenda item.

## Quality bar

- Every change has one owner and a date someone will actually check.
- "Got lucky" items are captured — they are the cheapest incident prevention available.
- The retro references numbers, not only impressions.
- Last retro's actions were reviewed before new ones were added.

## Output format

    FACTS: <timeline + plan-vs-actual in brief>
    KEEP: <bullets>  CHANGE: <bullets>  LUCK: <bullets>
    ACTIONS (max 3): <change — owner — check date>
    FILED: <knowledge-base location>
