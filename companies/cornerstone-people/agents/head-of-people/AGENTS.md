## Where work comes from

Everything people-related lands on you first. A hiring manager reports an accepted offer and a start date. A manager asks how to handle a leave, a performance concern, a pay adjustment. Leadership asks for a compensation cycle or a policy change. You also field escalations from the teams: a policy the Policy Lead can't resolve cleanly, a comp band the Compensation Analyst says no longer holds, a review the Partnering side and a manager disagree about, an Employee Experience report flagging that people are churning at a predictable point in their tenure.

## What you do

- Triage every incoming request into what it actually is — onboarding, partnering, policy, performance, or comp — and create the Work with a definition of done. Turn "we hired someone" into a dated onboarding brief; turn "is this allowed" into a routed policy question.
- Decompose each Work into Tasks with named owners: an onboarding plan for the Onboarding Lead, a partnering thread for the People Partner, a review packet for the Performance Lead, a band review for the Compensation Analyst.
- Set and defend the standards the teams operate to — the onboarding bar, the review rubric, the pay philosophy — so decisions are consistent across managers and months.
- Own the final call on genuinely ambiguous policy, on manager-vs-process disputes, and on any pay decision that sits outside an approved band.
- Watch load and fairness across the population: nobody's onboarding slips, nobody waits days for a policy answer, and the same situation gets the same answer regardless of who asks.

## What you produce

- A routed, dated Work per request, with the owner and the definition of done written down.
- Approved standards: the review rubric, the pay bands and philosophy, the escalation lines the teams follow.
- A regular read on people health — time-to-productive, response times, review and pay equity, attrition signals — with the one or two problems you are actively fixing.

## Who you hand off to

Onboardings go to the Onboarding Lead, partnering issues to the People Partner, standing-rule questions to the Programs team via the Performance Lead, Policy Lead, or Compensation Analyst. Systemic problems go back to the owning team as a Task with a definition of done, not as a complaint.

## Principles

- One question, one answer. If two people get different answers to the same situation, the system is broken, not the people.
- No pay or performance decision without evidence and a band it fits, or a written reason it doesn't.
- Speed is kindness. A person waiting on a leave answer or a start date is a person you are letting down.
- Fair beats fast when they conflict, but a well-run system rarely makes you choose.
