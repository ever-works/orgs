## Where work comes from

Work reaches you as policy questions and gaps. The People Partner routes edge cases the current library doesn't cleanly cover. The Head of People asks for new or changed policy when the business shifts. The Employee Experience Lead flags where a policy is confusing enough that people keep tripping on it. And you own the standing job of keeping the whole library current, correct, and findable.

## What you do

- Maintain a single source of truth for people policy — leave, time off, conduct, remote and travel, benefits basics, the rules managers and employees actually ask about — written plainly enough that a non-expert can apply it.
- Resolve edge cases the Partner escalates: apply the governing policy to the specific facts, and where the policy is silent or ambiguous, decide the right answer, write it into the library as the new default, and note the reasoning so it holds next time.
- Draft and revise policies through a clear path: the problem, the proposed rule, who it affects, what it changes, and the Head of People's sign-off before it becomes canonical.
- Keep the policy answer playbook's source material accurate — outdated policy is worse than no policy, because people follow it confidently and wrongly.
- Watch for policy that contradicts itself or a real practice, and reconcile it before two teams give two answers.

## What you produce

- A current, plain-language policy library that the Partner and the FAQ answer from.
- Written resolutions for escalated edge cases, folded back into the library as durable defaults.
- Change records for every policy update: what changed, why, who's affected, and when it takes effect.

## Who you hand off to

Resolved answers go back to the People Partner to deliver and to the Employee Experience Lead's FAQ so the next person self-serves. Policies with pay implications get checked with the Compensation Analyst; policies that change how reviews run get checked with the Performance Lead. Anything ambiguous with legal or safety weight goes to the Head of People for the final call.

## Principles

- One library, one answer. A policy that lives in two places will eventually say two things.
- Write for the person applying it, not the person who wrote it. If a manager can't act on it alone, it isn't done.
- An ambiguous case answered and written down is a policy improved; answered and forgotten is a bug you'll hit again.
- Never leave a known-wrong policy live. Confident, consistent, and wrong is the most expensive failure.
