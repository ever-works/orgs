Cornerstone exists to make the employee lifecycle run like a system instead of a scramble. A manager should be able to say "we hired someone, start date the third" or "my report is up for a raise" or "what's our policy on this," and get back a complete, consistent, on-time answer without chasing anyone. Every request enters through the Head of People, who decides what it is — an onboarding, a partnering issue, a policy question, a performance cycle, a comp review — and turns it into a Work with a clear owner and definition of done. The vague ask becomes a written brief; the brief becomes Tasks hung off the Work, each with a named owner and a date.

Three teams carry the lifecycle. Onboarding, led by the Onboarding Lead, takes an accepted hire and makes the first ninety days deliberate: a 30/60/90 plan, the accounts and access and paperwork the People Operations Coordinator drives to done, and a welcome that makes someone glad they said yes. Partnering, led by the People Partner, is the front door for everyone already here — managers and employees bring questions, tensions, and life events, and the Employee Experience Lead watches the signals across the whole population so recurring friction gets fixed at the source, not one ticket at a time. Programs, led by the Performance & Development Lead, owns the standing machinery — performance cycles built on the review packet, the policy library the Policy Lead keeps current, and the compensation bands the Compensation Analyst reviews so pay decisions are consistent and defensible.

Work moves in one direction with review on the way out. Onboarding hands a settled employee to Partnering once the 30/60/90 plan is underway. Partnering routes anything that touches a standing rule — a policy edge case, a pay question, a review dispute — to Programs, and Programs hands back an answer that becomes the reusable default, logged so the next person with the same question gets the same answer. Every handoff happens as an update on the Work's Tasks, so the trail from offer-accepted to fully-ramped, or from question to settled policy, stays intact. The Head of People owns the final call when a policy is genuinely ambiguous, when a manager and the process disagree, or when the experience data says the system itself needs to change.
