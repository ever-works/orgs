# Storyboard

A storyboard is the cheapest version of the finished piece: key frames plus intent, reviewable in minutes. Its job is to get the direction argument over with before rendering makes changes expensive.

## Building the storyboard

1. **Start from the message.** One sentence: what the viewer should understand or feel by the end. Write it at the top of the board — every frame is judged against it.
2. **Find the beats.** Break the piece into 3–8 moments where something changes for the viewer: a reveal, a transformation, a settle. Each beat becomes one key frame.
3. **Draw or describe each key frame.** A rough sketch, a composed still, or a precise textual description all work — the standard is that a reviewer can picture the frame, not that it's pretty.
4. **Caption each frame with one line of intent**: what the viewer understands *at this moment*. If you can't write the line, the frame has no job — cut it.
5. **Mark timing.** Approximate seconds per beat and total runtime. The first two seconds get their own scrutiny: they carry the message; everything after is reinforcement.
6. **Note the mechanics per frame**: camera or view movement, the dominant motion, and any effect technique the frame depends on — enough for the shot plan to be derived directly from the board.
7. **Flag the risk frame.** The one beat you're least sure can be produced as drawn. It gets prototyped first in production.

## Review pass before production

- Read only the captions, in order. Do they tell the story without the pictures? If not, the sequence logic is broken.
- Check the message lands with sound off and at small size — assume the worst viewing conditions.
- Confirm total runtime fits the brief's constraint; cut beats rather than compressing all of them.

## Quality bar

- One page or screen per storyboard; a board that needs scrolling to grasp is a script, not a board.
- Every frame has a caption; every caption advances the message.
- A reviewer can approve or redirect the piece from the board alone.

## Output format

Ordered key frames (image or description), each with: frame number, one-line intent caption, timing, mechanics note. Message sentence at top, total runtime and risk-frame flag at bottom.
