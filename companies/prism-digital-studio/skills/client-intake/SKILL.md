# Client Intake

Intake converts "we need an app" into a document a specialist can build against without guessing. The output is always a one-page brief; the input is usually a paragraph of hope.

## Running intake

1. **Read the request twice.** First pass for what they asked; second pass for what they are trying to cause. The gap between the two is where your questions live.
2. **Classify the work.** Application, visual media, document, or a package spanning several. Packages get one intake pass but will become multiple briefs — one per specialist deliverable.
3. **Ask the four anchor questions** (on the Task, in one batch — do not drip-feed):
   - Who is the audience, and what should they do after encountering the deliverable?
   - What does the client already have (brand assets, copy, data, existing systems) that this must fit?
   - What are the hard constraints — deadline, formats, platforms, things that must or must not appear?
   - How will the client judge this done? Push past "when it looks good" to something checkable.
4. **Draft the brief** with these sections: Objective (one sentence), Audience (three lines), Deliverables (name, format, and acceptance check per item), Constraints, Definition of done, Open assumptions (anything you inferred rather than confirmed, flagged).
5. **Confirm assumptions** with the client in one round. Unconfirmed assumptions stay flagged in the brief — the specialist deserves to know which ground is soft.
6. **Version it.** v1 at sign-off; any later change is a new version with a one-line changelog.

## Quality bar

- One page. A brief nobody reads protects nobody.
- Every deliverable has an acceptance check a stranger could apply.
- Zero load-bearing adjectives: "modern and clean" is a mood, not an instruction.
- If two deliverables serve different objectives, split into two briefs.

## Output format

Markdown, one page: version/date line, the six sections above, and a sign-off line. Attach to the Task and hand to the Studio Lead for routing.
