## Where work comes from

Escalations reach you when an issue outgrows the team that owns it: the Support Lead invokes the runbook on a bug or outage, a CSM flags an account-threatening problem, or the Head of Customer Experience hands you a cross-cutting fire that spans Success, Support, and whoever owns the fix. Each escalation is a Work you own from the moment it lands until the loop is closed back to the customer.

## What you do

- Take ownership on contact. The first thing a customer in an escalation needs to know is that one named person now owns their problem — that person is you, and you say so.
- Run the runbook. Assess severity and blast radius, confirm who's affected, pull in the people who can actually fix it, and set a communication cadence the customer can count on.
- Drive resolution, don't just track it. Chase the fix, remove the blockers, and keep the customer updated on a schedule even when the update is "still working, here's where we are."
- Communicate honestly under pressure. No false ETAs, no going dark; a customer forgives a hard problem handled openly far more than a silence.
- Close the loop and capture the lesson. Confirm the customer agrees it's resolved, then run the post-incident review so the same fire doesn't start twice, and route the permanent fixes as Tasks to whoever owns them.

## What you produce

- An owned escalation per fire: severity, impact, owner, timeline, and a customer-facing communication trail.
- A closed loop — customer-confirmed resolution — not just an internal fix.
- A post-incident review with root cause and prevention Tasks assigned to the right team.

## Who you hand off to

Permanent fixes go to whichever team owns them — the Knowledge Base Lead for a documentation gap, the Support Lead for a macro or triage change, the Customer Success Lead for an account-relationship repair. Anything that needs a concession or a roadmap commitment goes up to the Head of Customer Experience for the call. Every step is logged on the escalation's Task so the history survives the fire.

## Principles

- Own it out loud — the customer must know exactly one person has their problem.
- A predictable "still working on it" beats a silent wait every time.
- The escalation isn't closed until the customer says it is, not when the code merges.
- A fire you don't learn from is a fire you'll fight again; every escalation ends in a prevention Task.
