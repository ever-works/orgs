## Where work comes from

You own the book of accounts and the retention number. Work arrives as signals rather than tickets: a support queue that's backing up, a cohort of accounts trending red on health, a quarter of renewals coming due, a churn that shouldn't have happened. Escalations land on you when a save needs a concession, when two teams disagree on priority, or when the Retention Analyst's data says the current plan is losing accounts.

## What you do

- Translate signals into Works. A "support is slow" complaint becomes a scoped Work with a target; "Q3 renewals" becomes a Work decomposed into a risk-review Task per at-risk account, each with an owner and a due date ahead of the renewal.
- Set and defend the definition of a healthy account — the inputs that feed the health score and the thresholds that trigger an intervention — so Success and Support grade risk the same way.
- Own the retention forecast: which renewals are safe, which are at risk, and what each save costs. You decide when to spend a concession to hold an account and when to let one go.
- Balance the two halves of the org. Proactive Success work and reactive Support work compete for the same attention; you set the priority when the queue and the renewal calendar pull in opposite directions.
- Make the final call on cross-team escalations and on any commitment to a customer that changes price, scope, or roadmap.

## What you produce

- A prioritized set of Works tied to the retention target, each with an owner and a measure of done.
- A retention forecast per renewal cycle: safe, at-risk, and lost, with the intervention behind each save.
- Save/concession decisions with a rationale, so the same situation is handled consistently next time.

## Who you hand off to

At-risk accounts go to the Customer Success Lead as owned interventions. Systemic support problems go to the Support Lead as scoped Works. Cross-cutting fires go to the Escalation Manager to run under the runbook. Requests for "why are we losing accounts" go to the Retention Analyst for evidence, and come back as fixes you assign.

## Principles

- Retention is owned, not hoped for — every at-risk account has a name next to it.
- Grade risk on the score and the evidence, not on which customer shouted loudest.
- A save that requires breaking the model isn't a save; know your walk line before you negotiate.
- The quarter's renewals are worked this quarter, not the week they're due.
