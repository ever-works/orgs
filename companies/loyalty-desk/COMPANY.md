Loyalty Desk exists to keep customers — not by accident, but by system. Retention is treated as the sum of hundreds of small, owned moments: a health score that dipped, a ticket that took too long, a feature that never got adopted, a renewal that nobody prepared for. Everything enters through the Head of Customer Experience, who owns the book of accounts and the retention target. Incoming signals — a support queue that's backing up, a batch of accounts trending red, a quarter of renewals coming due — become Works, and each is decomposed into Tasks with a single named owner and a clear definition of done. Nothing that touches a customer is left unassigned.

Three teams carry the work. Success, led by the Customer Success Lead, owns the proactive side: the Customer Success Manager drives adoption, scores account health, runs the business reviews, and prepares every renewal before it's due. Support, led by the Support Lead, owns the reactive side: the Support Agent resolves tickets fast and correctly using a shared macro library, and the Knowledge Base Lead turns each resolved issue into a help article so the help center absorbs volume the queue would otherwise carry. Operations, led by the Escalation Manager, owns the cross-cutting layer: the Escalation Manager runs the runbook when an issue jumps a team or threatens an account, and the Retention Analyst measures the whole system — surfacing renewal risk and telling the org where accounts are actually leaking.

Work flows in one direction with review gates on the way out. A support ticket is resolved, then reviewed for a knowledge gap before it's closed; a red health score becomes an owned intervention, not a note in a spreadsheet; a renewal is worked from a written risk review, not a last-week scramble. Every handoff — Support flagging an at-risk account to Success, Success escalating a stuck issue to Operations, Operations closing the loop back to the customer — happens as an update on the account's or ticket's Task, so the trail stays intact. The Head of Customer Experience owns the final call when a save requires a concession, when teams disagree on priority, or when the retention data says the plan has to change.
