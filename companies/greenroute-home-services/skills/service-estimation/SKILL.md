# Service Estimation

An estimate is a promise with a number on it. This playbook produces estimates fast enough to win the job and accurate enough to survive it.

## The SLA comes first

- The clock starts when the customer submits the request, not when it is noticed. Log the request timestamp immediately.
- Target: estimate delivered within the stated SLA window. A fast, slightly conservative estimate beats a perfect one that arrives after the customer hired someone else — responsiveness is itself a selling point in local services.
- If the SLA cannot be met (site visit genuinely required, missing critical detail), the customer hears that within the SLA window instead, with a specific date for the real number.

## Qualify before pricing

Three checks, in order, before any math:

1. **In scope?** Service offered, property inside the service area. Out-of-area or out-of-scope requests get a same-day polite decline — a fast no also protects reputation.
2. **Enough information?** Every service line has a short list of required inputs (size or count, access, condition, frequency). Missing inputs trigger one consolidated question to the customer — never a drip of one-question messages.
3. **Red flags?** Hazards, disputed property lines, requests to skip safety steps. Flag to the Operations Director before quoting.

## Build the number

- Price from measurable drivers: area or unit count × the service-line base rate, adjusted by the condition and access multipliers on record. Measure remotely where possible (mapping tools, customer photos) instead of defaulting to a site visit.
- Apply the current correction factors from the estimation-accuracy review — these encode every past week where quotes ran hot or cold.
- One-off jobs carry a wider stated range; recurring services quote the per-visit rate and the visit cadence explicitly.
- Note travel economics: a job far outside current route clusters either carries the drive-time premium or is offered the "next time we're in your area" discount date. Never silently eat the drive.

## Deliver

The estimate the customer sees contains: the price (or bounded range), exactly what is included, what would change the price, the validity window, and the single next step to book. The cover note comes from the communications lead — the estimator supplies the facts, not the prose.

## Close the loop

After every completed job, record actual time and cost next to the estimate. The revenue analyst reviews the deltas quarterly and issues updated correction factors. An estimation playbook without this loop drifts into fiction within a season.
