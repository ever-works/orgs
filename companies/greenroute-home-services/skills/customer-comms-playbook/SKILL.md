# Customer Communications Playbook

Every message a customer reads is the company's handshake. This playbook keeps that handshake identical no matter which agent triggered the message.

## Voice

- Warm, direct, human. Short sentences. The customer's own words for their problem.
- Concrete over corporate: "We'll be there Tuesday between 9 and 11" — never "your service window has been scheduled".
- No exclamation inflation, no apology padding, no "we strive to". One sincere sentence outworks three reflexive ones.
- Read-aloud test: if the sentence would sound odd said face-to-face on the customer's doorstep, rewrite it.

## The template library

Operational messages come from templates; custom prose is the exception. The core set:

- **Estimate cover note** — the price framed with what is included and one clear booking step.
- **Booking confirmation** — date, window, what to expect, what the customer should do (if anything).
- **Day-before reminder** and **on-our-way notice** — short, channel: text.
- **Job-complete summary** — what was done, anything noticed worth flagging, the review ask when goodwill is highest.
- **Quote follow-up** — sent on a fixed cadence when a quote goes silent; each touch adds one new piece of useful information rather than repeating the ask.
- **Seasonal re-engagement** — timed by the demand forecast, offered before the season peaks.
- **Payment reminder** — factual, friendly, escalating only in specificity, never in tone.

Template changes are versioned with a note on why. A template that keeps needing manual edits is a rewrite signal, not a nuisance.

## Channel rules

- Email for anything the customer may need to find again: estimates, confirmations, receipts.
- Text for day-of logistics only — reminders, arrival notices, small delays.
- Complaints are answered on the channel they arrived on.
- Quiet hours are respected for outbound texts, no exceptions.

## Complaints: the two-beat structure

1. **Beat one — same day.** Name the specific problem in the customer's terms, say what happens next, and attach a time to it. No defense, no explanation of internal causes, no promise beyond what operations confirmed.
2. **Beat two — close the loop.** After the fix, state what was actually done and ask one question: is this resolved for you?

Silence between the beats is the most common way companies turn a fixable complaint into a lost customer. If the fix slips, the customer hears about the slip before the deadline passes.

## Escalation line

Any message that commits money, admits fault, or changes policy goes to the Operations Director as a draft before sending. Everything else ships on the author's judgment.
