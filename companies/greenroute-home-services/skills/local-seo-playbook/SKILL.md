# Local SEO Playbook

For a home-services company, local search is the storefront. This playbook runs visibility as a monthly operating cycle, not a one-time project.

## The visibility baseline

Maintain a small, honest scorecard and re-measure it monthly:

- Rankings for each target query, where a target query is always *service + area* ("gutter cleaning + [neighborhood]") — never vanity head terms.
- Business-profile completeness: categories, services, hours, photos newer than ninety days, Q&A answered.
- Review count, average rating, and — most telling — review velocity versus last quarter.
- Citation consistency: name, address, and phone identical everywhere they appear.

The same scorecard doubles as a competitor yardstick: run it against a competitor to measure their footprint on equal terms.

## The monthly cycle

1. Re-measure the baseline; note movement and its likely cause.
2. Fix regressions first (a dropped ranking, a stale profile section, a citation drift) — recovery beats expansion.
3. Ship one improvement: a new service-area page, a batch of real job photos, a refreshed underperforming page.
4. Log what was done, so next month's movement can be attributed instead of guessed at.

## Service-area pages

- One page per *service + area* pair that the business genuinely serves and wants to win. No thin near-duplicates — a page exists because it answers a real question differently.
- Each page answers what a local customer actually asks before hiring: what the job involves, honest pricing expectations, timing, and what makes this operator worth calling. Straight talk about price ranges builds more trust than hiding the number.
- Real photos from real local jobs outperform stock imagery in both rankings and conversions. Caption them with the area and service.
- Every page carries the same clear next step: request a quote, with the SLA promise stated plainly — fast response time is a competitive claim worth advertising.

## The review engine

- Ask at the moment of highest goodwill: inside the job-complete message, with a direct link and a one-line ask.
- Respond to every review within the week. Positive: short, specific thanks. Negative: the same two-beat structure used for complaints — acknowledge specifically, state the fix, take the details offline.
- Never incentivize or gate reviews. Volume from genuinely asked customers compounds; manufactured volume eventually costs more than it earned.
- Mine review text quarterly: the words customers use are the words pages should use.

## What not to do

No purchased links, no keyword-stuffed doorway pages, no fake locations. In a small service area, shortcuts are both detectable and reputationally expensive — the durable inputs are proximity, reviews, and relevance, and only the last two can be worked.
