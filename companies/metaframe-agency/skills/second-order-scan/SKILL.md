# Second-Order Scan

Think past the first ripple. The failure mode this prevents: decisions that score well on immediate effects and quietly poison the system six months later — the discount that trains customers to wait, the metric that optimizes away quality.

## Steps

1. State the decision and its intended first-order effect in one line each.
2. Build the consequence tree, two levels minimum:
   - **First order**: what happens directly, within the first cycle.
   - **Second order**: for each first-order effect, ask "and then what?" — who reacts, what do they now do differently, what becomes cheaper or more expensive?
   - **Third order** only where a second-order branch is severe; deeper than three is usually speculation.
3. At each branch, name the actor who responds (customers, team, competitor, the system itself) — consequences come from responses, not from the decision floating in a vacuum.
4. Hunt specifically for the three classic patterns:
   - **Incentive shift**: who is now rewarded for different behavior?
   - **Feedback loop**: does any branch feed back to amplify or dampen the original effect?
   - **Slack consumption**: does this spend a buffer (trust, margin, capacity) that was invisibly absorbing shocks?
5. Rate each second-order branch: direction (helps/hurts), delay (when it lands), reversibility (can we undo once it appears).
6. Weigh: does the first-order gain survive subtracting the hurtful branches? State the answer explicitly — many decisions win at order one and lose at order two.
7. For hurtful-but-tolerated branches, define the early-warning signal and the date to check it.

## Quality bar

- Every first-order effect has at least one traced second-order branch; a bare tree means the scan stopped at advocacy.
- At least one branch involves someone gaming or adapting to the change.
- Irreversible hurtful branches get explicit sign-off, never silent tolerance.

## Output format

Consequence tree (indented list or diagram) with actor, direction, delay, reversibility per branch → the three-pattern findings → net verdict → watchlist of early-warning signals with check dates.
