# Leverage Audit

Find the vital few and the first domino. The failure mode this prevents: effort spread evenly across a long list where two items carry most of the value and one of them unlocks three others.

## Steps

1. Fix the outcome variable first: what is being maximized (revenue, time recovered, risk retired)? An audit without a named outcome ranks by loudness.
2. List every candidate action or problem — everything competing for the same capacity. Do not pre-filter; the audit exists to filter.
3. Estimate each item's contribution to the outcome variable. Rough orders of magnitude beat false precision: high / medium / low with a one-line justification each.
4. Sort and find the knee: typically 20-30% of items account for 70-80% of estimated contribution. Draw the line explicitly — items above are the *vital few*, below are the *useful many*.
5. Within the vital few, test for the first domino: which item, if done first, makes others easier, cheaper, or unnecessary? Check each vital-few item against the others: "if this were done, what happens to the rest?" The item with the most downstream unlocks leads.
6. Sanity-check the discards: scan the useful many for any item whose *absence* is catastrophic even though its contribution is small (compliance, a keep-the-lights-on dependency). Promote those to a small "must-not-drop" list — leverage ranking does not apply to table stakes.
7. Write the deferral note for everything below the line: not "cut", but "after the vital few, in this order, unless X changes".

## Quality bar

- One outcome variable; if two genuinely compete, run two audits and reconcile at the end.
- The vital few is at most a third of the list — a "vital most" means the estimates dodged hard calls.
- The first domino has its downstream unlocks named, not just asserted.

## Output format

Outcome statement → ranked table with contribution estimates and justifications → the line, the vital few, and the first domino with its unlock chain → must-not-drop list → deferral note for the rest.
